üëó Understanding Conforming Clothing

Let's start by understanding what makes conforming clothing special. It's one of those Poser features that seems like magic when you first use it, but understanding how it works helps you use it more effectively.

What Is Conforming Clothing?

Conforming clothing is 3D clothing that has been specially designed to automatically wrap around and follow a specific figure. Think of it like a second skin that knows the figure's skeletal structure and can bend, twist, and pose right along with it.

üîç The Key Difference

Aspect Regular Prop Conforming Clothing
Structure Single rigid object Rigged with bones matching figure
Movement Moves as one piece Bends and deforms with figure
Attachment Manual parenting required Conforms automatically
Posing Must reposition for each pose Follows figure poses automatically
Example Hat, sword, cup, chair Shirt, pants, dress, shoes

How Conforming Works: The Technical Magic

When you "conform" clothing to a figure, here's what happens behind the scenes:

üéØ The Key Concept: Matching Rigging

For clothing to conform, it must have been created with a rigging structure that matches the figure. A shirt designed for Poser's LaFemme figure has the same bone structure (shoulder, upper arm, forearm, hand) as LaFemme herself. When you conform it, each clothing bone aligns with its corresponding figure bone.

This is why you can't just take any 3D clothing from the internet and make it conform‚Äîit needs to be specifically designed for Poser and for the figure you're using.

Real-World Analogy

Think of conforming clothing like a puppet costume. Imagine a marionette puppet wearing a tiny jacket. The jacket isn't just glued to the puppet‚Äîit's designed with the same joints. When the puppet's arm bends at the elbow, the jacket's sleeve has an elbow too, so it bends naturally. That's exactly how conforming clothing works in 3D.

In contrast, a regular prop is like putting a cardboard box on the puppet. Sure, you can attach it, but it won't bend or deform‚Äîit stays rigid no matter what the puppet does.

üí° Why This Matters: Understanding that conforming clothing has its own skeleton helps you troubleshoot issues. When clothing doesn't follow a pose correctly, it's usually because the rigging doesn't perfectly match, or morphs are needed to adjust the fit. We'll tackle these problems later in this lesson!

üéΩ Types of Clothing and Wearables

Not all clothing in Poser works the same way. Let's break down the different types you'll encounter and how each behaves.

Conforming Clothing (The Main Event)

What It Includes

  • Body covering garments: Shirts, pants, dresses, skirts, jackets, coats
  • Footwear: Shoes, boots, sandals, socks
  • Full body items: Bodysuits, jumpsuits, full armor sets
  • Undergarments: Underwear, bras, slips (yes, layering matters!)

Behavior: Follows figure's skeleton automatically after conforming

Library location: Usually under Figures ‚Üí (Brand) ‚Üí Clothing

Smart Props

‚ú® The Automatic Helpers

Smart props are props (not clothing) that automatically parent themselves to the correct body part when loaded. They're "smart" because they know where they belong!

Common smart props:

  • Glasses/sunglasses: Auto-parent to head
  • Hats and headwear: Auto-parent to head
  • Jewelry: Necklaces (neck/chest), bracelets (hands/wrists), earrings (head)
  • Held items: Some weapons, tools auto-parent to hands
  • Hair: Technically a smart prop that parents to head

Behavior: Auto-parent on load, move with that body part, but don't bend/deform

Library location: Props ‚Üí (Brand) ‚Üí Accessories or Smart Props folder

Dynamic Clothing

üí® Physics-Based Clothing (Advanced)

Dynamic clothing uses physics simulation to drape and flow realistically. Instead of conforming to the figure, it calculates how fabric would actually behave with gravity, wind, and movement.

Examples: Flowing capes, long dresses, flags, curtains

When to use: When you need realistic fabric draping and flow

Complexity: More advanced‚Äîrequires cloth simulation setup

Note: We'll cover basic dynamic cloth in advanced lessons. For now, focus on conforming clothing!

Rigid Props (Non-Conforming)

The Traditional Props

Regular props that you manually parent to figures, like we learned in Lesson 10.

Examples: Weapons, bags, tools, cups, books

Behavior: Rigid, move as one piece, require manual parenting

When to use: For items that don't need to deform (hard objects)

Compatibility: Figure-Specific Clothing

‚ö†Ô∏è Critical Rule: Clothing Must Match Figure

Conforming clothing is designed for specific figures. You cannot mix and match freely between different figure types without issues.

Examples of figure compatibility:

  • LaFemme clothing works on LaFemme figures
  • Andy clothing works on Andy figures (the male equivalent)
  • Genesis 8 clothing works on Genesis 8 figures (DAZ content)
  • Victoria 4 clothing works on Victoria 4 figures (older generation)

What happens if you try wrong clothing?

  • Won't conform properly
  • Wrong size (huge or tiny)
  • Won't follow poses correctly
  • Massive poke-through and clipping

Solution: Always check clothing description for compatible figures before purchasing or using!

üí° Shopping Tip: When buying clothing from content stores, the product page always lists compatible figures. Make sure your figure is on that list! Many clothing sets work with multiple figure generations, but never assume‚Äîalways check.

üëï Loading and Conforming Clothing

Now let's get practical. Here's exactly how to dress your figure with conforming clothing.

The Complete Clothing Workflow

Step-by-Step: Dressing Your Figure

  1. Start with your posed figure
    • Have your figure loaded and posed (or use default pose)
    • Figure should be selected in scene
  2. Navigate to clothing in library
    • Open Library palette
    • Go to Figures ‚Üí (Your Figure Brand) ‚Üí Clothing
    • Or Props ‚Üí Clothing (organization varies)
  3. Preview the clothing item
    • Click on clothing thumbnail to see preview
    • Check if it matches your figure type
  4. Load the clothing
    • Double-click clothing item
    • Or drag into scene
    • Clothing loads at origin, usually overlapping figure
  5. Conform the clothing to figure
    • With clothing selected
    • Go to: Figure menu ‚Üí Conform To...
    • Select your figure from dropdown list
    • Click OK
  6. Watch the magic happen
    • Clothing wraps itself around figure
    • Automatically matches figure's current pose
    • Becomes child of figure in hierarchy
  7. Adjust if needed
    • Check for poke-through
    • Apply morphs to fix fit (covered next section)

Auto-Conform vs. Manual Conform

Some clothing items are set to auto-conform when loaded. This means step 5 above happens automatically‚Äîthe clothing conforms immediately upon loading. Other times, you need to manually conform using the Figure menu.

‚úÖ How to Tell If Clothing Is Conformed

  • Visual check: Clothing wraps naturally around figure, follows pose
  • Hierarchy check: Look at hierarchy panel
    • Conformed: Clothing appears nested under figure
    • Not conformed: Clothing is separate in hierarchy
  • Pose test: Move figure's arm
    • Conformed: Clothing sleeve moves with arm
    • Not conformed: Clothing stays in place, arm pokes through

Common Loading Scenarios

Scenario 1: Auto-Conform Clothing

What happens:

  • Double-click clothing in library
  • Clothing loads AND automatically conforms to active figure
  • No manual conform step needed

This is ideal! Many modern clothing sets work this way.

Scenario 2: Manual Conform Required

What happens:

  • Double-click clothing in library
  • Clothing loads but doesn't conform automatically
  • Appears overlapping figure but not following pose

Solution:

  1. Select clothing item
  2. Figure ‚Üí Conform To ‚Üí Select Figure ‚Üí OK

Scenario 3: Wrong Figure Selected

What happens:

  • You have multiple figures in scene
  • Load clothing
  • It conforms to wrong figure (or random one)

Solution:

  1. Select clothing
  2. Figure ‚Üí Conform To ‚Üí Select CORRECT figure ‚Üí OK
  3. Clothing recon forms to proper figure

Understanding the Conform Dialog

  • Grayed out figures: Incompatible with this clothing
  • Multiple matches: If you have multiple of same figure type, all appear

Unconforming Clothing

Sometimes you need to unconform clothing (turn it back into a regular prop):

  • When: If you want to parent it differently or it's not working right
  • How: Select clothing ‚Üí Figure ‚Üí Unconf orm
  • Result: Clothing becomes independent prop, stops following figure
  • Then: Can delete it, reconform to different figure, or manually parent

üí° Pro Tip: Before conforming clothing, make sure your figure is selected and that it's the figure you want! With multiple figures in a scene, clothing can auto-conform to the wrong one. If that happens, simply select the clothing and use Figure ‚Üí Conform To to switch it to the correct figure.

üîß Adjusting Fit with Morphs

Even with perfect conforming, clothing rarely fits absolutely perfectly right out of the box. This is where clothing morphs save the day. Think of morphs as the tailoring tools of the 3D world.

Why Fit Adjustments Are Needed

Several factors can cause fit issues:

Common Fit Problems

  • Poke-through: Body parts (breasts, shoulders, knees) poke through clothing
  • Figure morphs: If you morphed your figure (taller, more muscular, different body shape), clothing may not match
  • Extreme poses: Very bent limbs or twisted torsos can cause clothing to pull or clip
  • Layering: Wearing clothing over other clothing requires the outer layer to be slightly bigger
  • Different figure versions: Even within same figure type, proportions can vary

Types of Clothing Morphs

üéöÔ∏è Fit Morphs (The Most Important)

These adjust how closely clothing hugs the figure or how loose it is:

  • Tightness/Looseness: Make clothing tighter or looser overall
  • Body part specific:
    • Bust adjust (for shirts, dresses)
    • Hips adjust
    • Shoulders adjust
    • Arm/leg thickness
  • Smooth/Tighten: Pull clothing closer to body to eliminate poke-through
  • Expand/Thicken: Make clothing bigger for layering

‚ú® Style Morphs

These change the appearance of the clothing itself:

  • Sleeve length: Short sleeve ‚Üî Long sleeve
  • Hem length: Short skirt ‚Üî Long skirt
  • Collar: Open ‚Üî Closed, high collar ‚Üî low collar
  • Details: Pockets visible, buttons open/closed, rolled sleeves
  • Damage: Torn, worn, battle-damaged

üîÑ Matching Morphs (The Helper Morphs)

These match specific figure body morphs:

  • What they are: If your figure uses "BodyBuilder" morph, clothing may have matching "BodyBuilder" morph
  • Purpose: Automatically adjust clothing to match figure's shape changes
  • How to use: If you morphed your figure, look for matching morphs in clothing
  • Common matching morphs: Muscular, Thin, Heavy, Tall, Short, Breast Size variations

How to Access and Use Clothing Morphs

Step-by-Step: Applying Morphs

  1. Select the clothing item
    • Click on clothing in viewport
    • Or select in hierarchy panel
  2. Open Parameters/Properties panel
    • Window ‚Üí Parameters (if not visible)
    • Usually on right side of interface
  3. Find morph category
    • Look for "Morphs" folder/category
    • May be under "Body" or specific body parts
    • Sometimes organized as "Fit", "Style", "Adjustments"
  4. Identify the morph you need
    • Read morph names carefully
    • Look for descriptive names (BustAdjust, TightenChest, etc.)
  5. Adjust the morph dial
    • Move slider or type in value
    • Watch viewport to see effect
    • Typical range: -1.0 to 1.0 or 0% to 100%
  6. Make small adjustments
    • Start with small values (0.1 to 0.3)
    • Increase gradually until problem fixed
    • Too much can look unnatural
  7. Combine multiple morphs if needed
    • May need bust adjust + tighten chest together
    • Each morph targets specific area

Common Morph Solutions

Problem Morphs to Try Typical Values
Breasts poking through shirt Bust Adjust, Expand Bust, Smooth Bust +0.2 to +0.5
Shoulders clipping Shoulder Width, Smooth Shoulders +0.1 to +0.3

Pro Tips for Morph Adjustment

üí° Expert Morphing Techniques

  • Work from different camera angles: Poke-through might be hidden from one angle but visible from another
  • Pose first, fit second: Get your pose right, THEN adjust clothing. Morphs needed depend on pose
  • Less is more: Over-morphing makes clothing look wrong. Start small (0.1-0.2)
  • Body-part specific: Most clothing lets you morph individual body parts separately‚Äîuse this!
  • Save morph combinations: If you find a good combination, note the values for future use
  • Test renders: What looks fine in viewport might show problems in render (or vice versa)
  • Layer strategically: Inner layer tight, outer layers progressively looser

‚ö†Ô∏è When Morphs Aren't Enough

Sometimes morphs can't fix severe issues:

  • Completely wrong figure: Clothing designed for different figure‚Äîget correct clothing
  • Extreme body morphs: If you made figure drastically different, clothing may not have enough morphs
  • Poor quality clothing: Some clothing just isn't well-made‚Äîlimited morph options
  • Extreme pose limits: Some poses break any clothing (yoga positions, etc.)

Solutions:

  • Try different clothing that fits better
  • Reduce figure body morphs slightly
  • Use less extreme poses
  • Hide problem areas with camera angles
  • Advanced: Use cloth room for dynamic simulation

üéØ The Morphing Mindset: Think of morphs like a tailor making adjustments. A little tuck here, a little let-out there. You're not redesigning the clothing‚Äîyou're fine-tuning the fit. Small, strategic adjustments usually solve 90% of fit issues!

üé© Smart Props and Auto-Parenting

Now let's talk about smart props‚Äîthose clever accessories that know exactly where they belong on your figure without you having to manually parent them!

What Makes Props "Smart"?

A smart prop is a regular prop with built-in parenting information. The creator embedded instructions that tell Poser: "When loaded, automatically parent me to [specific body part]."

Smart Props vs. Regular Props

Feature Regular Prop Smart Prop
Loading Loads at origin (0,0,0) Loads already attached to body part
Parenting Must manually parent Auto-parents on load
Position Must manually position Pre-positioned (usually correct spot)
Movement Stays at origin until parented Immediately follows body part
Examples Generic sword, cup, book Glasses, hats, earrings, hair

Common Smart Props

‚ú® Typical Smart Props You'll Encounter

Head-Parenting Items:

  • Glasses/Sunglasses: Auto-parent to head, positioned on nose bridge
  • Hats/Caps/Helmets: Parent to head, sit on top of head
  • Earrings: Parent to head, positioned at ears
  • Headbands/Tiaras: Parent to head
  • Hair: Technically a smart prop (even though treated specially)

Neck/Chest Items:

  • Necklaces: Parent to neck or chest
  • Ties: Parent to neck/chest
  • Scarves: Parent to neck

Hand/Wrist Items:

  • Bracelets/Watches: Parent to left or right wrist
  • Rings: Parent to specific fingers
  • Gloves: Sometimes smart props, sometimes conforming

Held Items:

  • Weapons: Some smart-parent to hands
  • Microphones: Parent to hand
  • Phones: Parent to hand

How to Use Smart Props

The Easy Process

  1. Make sure figure is loaded (smart prop needs a parent!)
  2. Select your figure (smart prop will parent to active figure if multiple in scene)
  3. Navigate to smart prop in library
    • Usually in: Props ‚Üí [Brand] ‚Üí Accessories
    • Or: Props ‚Üí Smart Props
    • Or alongside conforming clothing
  4. Double-click to load
  5. Smart prop automatically:
    • Parents to correct body part
    • Positions itself correctly
    • Ready to use immediately!
  6. Fine-tune position if needed
    • Translate slightly for perfect fit
    • Rotate if not quite right
    • Scale if size is off

When Smart Props Need Adjustments

Smart props are pre-positioned for the default figure. If you've morphed your figure or used a different pose, adjustments may be needed:

Common Adjustments

Glasses:

  • May need Z-translate (forward/back) to sit on nose properly
  • May need scale if head is morphed larger/smaller
  • May need slight rotation if head is tilted

Hats:

  • May need Y-translate (up/down) to sit at right height
  • May need scale for different head sizes
  • May sink into hair‚Äîadjust positioning

Jewelry:

  • Necklaces may need length adjustment (Y-translate)
  • Bracelets may need scale for different wrist sizes
  • Earrings usually perfect as-is

Smart Props and Multiple Figures

‚ö†Ô∏è Important: Which Figure Gets the Smart Prop?

If you have multiple figures in your scene, smart props will parent to the currently selected figure.

Best practice:

  1. Select the specific figure you want to dress/accessorize
  2. Then load smart props
  3. They'll attach to that figure

If smart prop attaches to wrong figure:

  1. Select the smart prop
  2. Object ‚Üí Change Parent
  3. Select correct body part on correct figure
  4. May need to reposition

Hair: The Special Smart Prop

Hair in Poser is technically a smart prop, but it's special enough to warrant its own mention:

üíá Working with Hair Props

  • Location: Usually in Hair library category (not Props)
  • Loading: Auto-parents to head like other smart props
  • Fitting: Often has morphs to adjust fit, length, style

‚ú® The Smart Props Advantage: Smart props save you time and frustration. Instead of manually parenting and positioning every accessory, you get instant results. The Loading Order Strategy

Load clothing in layer order‚Äîinnermost to outermost. This helps prevent issues and makes adjustments easier.

‚úÖ Proper Loading Sequence

  1. Start with figure (obviously!)
  2. Load undergarments (if visible or if affecting outer layers)
    • Bra, underwear, undershirt
    • Conform to figure
  3. Load base clothing layer
    • Shirt, pants, skirt, dress
    • Conform to figure
    • Apply morphs to fit over undergarments
  4. Load mid layers (if any)
    • Vests, sweaters, casual jackets
    • Conform to figure
    • Apply morphs to fit over base layer
  5. Load outer layers
    • Heavy jackets, coats, cloaks
    • Conform to figure
    • Apply morphs to fit over everything
  6. Add accessories
    • Belts, scarves, jewelry
    • Smart props and regular props
  7. Finally, add hair (interacts with clothing collars/hoods)

Morphing for Layers

Each outer layer needs to be slightly bigger than the layer beneath it. This is where layer morphs become essential:

Layer-Specific Morph Applications

Undergarments (Layer 1):

  • Should be tight/form-fitting
  • May need tighten morphs
  • Minimal adjustments usually needed

Base Clothing (Layer 2):

  • Needs to fit over underwear
  • Apply expand/loosen morphs: +0.1 to +0.2
  • Focus on areas where underwear is thick

Mid Layers (Layer 3):

  • Must fit over shirt/pants
  • Apply expand/thicken morphs: +0.2 to +0.4
  • May need armhole adjustments for sleeves

Outer Layers (Layer 4):

  • Must accommodate everything underneath
  • Apply expand morphs: +0.3 to +0.6
  • Often come with "layering" morphs specifically for this

Common Layering Scenarios

Scenario 1: T-Shirt Under Jacket

Process:

  1. Load and conform t-shirt ‚Üí fit normally
  2. Load and conform jacket
  3. On jacket: apply "Expand" or "Thicken" morph (+0.3 to +0.5)
  4. Check sleeves: may need "Arm Thickness" adjustment
  5. Check shoulders: may need "Shoulder Width" adjustment
  6. Result: Jacket sits naturally over shirt

Scenario 2: Pants Over Underwear

Process:

  1. Load and conform underwear ‚Üí fit normally
  2. Load and conform pants
  3. On pants: slight "Expand" morph (+0.1 to +0.2)
  4. Focus on hip and thigh area
  5. Underwear should not poke through waistband
  6. Result: Natural pants fit

Scenario 3: Full Winter Outfit

Process:

  1. Underwear ‚Üí conform, normal fit
  2. Long underwear/thermal layer ‚Üí conform, expand +0.1
  3. Shirt ‚Üí conform, expand +0.2
  4. Sweater ‚Üí conform, expand +0.3
  5. Heavy jacket ‚Üí conform, expand +0.5 to +0.7
  6. Result: Bulky winter look without clipping

The Hierarchy Panel: Your Layering Manager

The hierarchy panel shows you all clothing items and their relationship to the figure. Use it to manage complex outfits:

üìã Managing Layers in Hierarchy

  • Organization: All conformed clothing appears nested under figure
  • Naming: Rename each piece descriptively
    • Instead of: "Shirt", "Shirt 1", "Shirt 2"
    • Use: "T-Shirt", "Button-Shirt", "Jacket"
  • Visibility toggle: Click eye icon to hide layers temporarily
    • Hide outer layers to work on inner layers
    • Toggle on/off to compare
  • Selection: Click item in hierarchy to select it for morphing
  • Order awareness: Load order determines which items are "on top" visually

Troubleshooting Layer Issues

‚ö†Ô∏è Common Layering Problems

Problem: Inner layer poking through outer layer

Solution:

  • Increase outer layer's expand morph
  • OR decrease inner layer's tight/conform morph
  • Check specific problem areas (elbows, knees, shoulders)

Problem: Too much gap between layers (floating clothing)

Solution:

  • Reduce outer layer's expand morph slightly
  • Layers should be close but not clipping

Problem: Can't tell which layer is which

Solution:

  • Use hierarchy panel
  • Rename items clearly
  • Toggle visibility to isolate layers

Problem: Scene getting slow with many layers

Solution:

  • Hide body parts under clothing (see next section)
  • Lower preview quality
  • Work on layers separately, combine for final render

Layer Textures and Materials

Beyond fit, consider visual layering:

Visual Layering Tips

  • Color coordination: Layers should look intentional together
  • Texture variety: Mix smooth and textured fabrics for visual interest
  • Edge visibility: Inner layers should be visible at edges
    • Shirt collar visible above jacket neckline
    • Shirt cuffs visible beyond jacket sleeves
    • This proves layering is real
  • Material differences: Different shader properties for different materials
    • Cotton shirt: matte, less reflective
    • Leather jacket: slight gloss, more reflective

üëî The Layering Rule of Thumb: In the real world, we instinctively know a jacket goes over a shirt. Apply that same logic in 3D. Load in order, expand progressively, and check from multiple angles. If it looks wrong, it is wrong‚Äîtrust your eye and adjust until it looks natural!

üëÅÔ∏è Hiding Body Parts for Performance

Here's a pro technique that both improves performance and solves poke-through problems: hiding body parts that are completely covered by clothing.

Why Hide Body Parts?

Benefits of Hiding Covered Geometry

  • Performance improvement: Less geometry = faster viewport and faster renders
  • Eliminates poke-through: Can't poke through if body part isn't there!
  • Cleaner workflow: Less visual clutter when working
  • Simpler morph adjustments: Focus on visible clothing only
  • Professional technique: This is what the pros do!

What to Hide and When

‚úÖ Safe to Hide

Full Coverage Scenarios:

  • Long sleeve shirt: Hide arms, hands (if gloves too)
  • Long pants: Hide legs, feet (if shoes too)
  • Full body suit: Hide torso, arms, legs
  • Gloves: Hide hands
  • Shoes/Boots: Hide feet
  • Helmet with visor: Hide head (if completely covered)

Rule: If camera will NEVER see it, hide it!

‚ö†Ô∏è Don't Hide These

Visible Body Parts:

  • Exposed skin: Obvious‚Äîif it shows, it stays
  • Head/Face: Unless completely covered by helmet
  • Hands: If no gloves or sleeves ride up
  • Feet: If wearing sandals or going barefoot
  • Partial coverage: Short sleeves showing forearms

Warning: Don't hide body parts you might need to show from different camera angles!

How to Hide Body Parts

Method 1: Using the Hierarchy Panel (Easiest)

  1. Open hierarchy panel (usually on left side)
  2. Expand your figure to see body parts
  3. Find the body part to hide
    • Example: Left Forearm, Right Hand, Left Foot
  4. Click the eye icon next to that body part
    • Eye open = visible
    • Eye closed/crossed = hidden
  5. Repeat for other body parts
  6. Body parts remain hidden in viewport and renders

To unhide: Click the eye icon again to toggle visibility back on

Method 2: Using Properties Panel

  1. Select the body part (click on it in viewport or hierarchy)
  2. Open Properties panel (Window ‚Üí Properties)
  3. Find "Visible" checkbox
  4. Uncheck "Visible"
  5. Body part disappears

To unhide: Recheck the "Visible" checkbox

Strategic Hiding Workflow

üéØ Pro Hiding Strategy

  1. Dress figure completely first
    • Load all clothing
    • Conform everything
    • Apply necessary morphs
  2. Set final camera angle
    • Determines what's actually visible
    • Some body parts may be hidden by camera angle too
  3. Identify completely covered parts
    • Walk through figure mentally
    • Check from multiple angles (not just camera view)
  4. Hide conservatively first
    • Start with obviously hidden parts
    • Test render to ensure nothing shows that shouldn't
  5. Hide aggressively for performance
    • If confident about coverage, hide more
    • Can always unhide if needed

Common Hiding Patterns

Outfit Type Body Parts to Hide Notes
Business suit (full) Torso, arms (if long sleeves), legs, feet (in shoes) Keep hands visible unless wearing gloves
Casual jeans & t-shirt Legs (covered by jeans), feet (in shoes) Arms visible if short sleeves
Long dress Legs, feet Arms depend on sleeve length
Full armor Everything except head (if visible) Most aggressive hiding scenario
Swimsuit None (minimal coverage) Most skin visible
Winter coat Torso, arms, legs (if long), feet Heavy clothing = more hiding opportunity

The Performance Impact

How much does hiding body parts help?

Performance Comparison

  • Full figure visible + clothing: 100% geometry load
  • Hide arms and legs: ~40-50% reduction
  • Hide torso, arms, legs: ~60-70% reduction
  • Full armor (most hidden): ~70-80% reduction

Result: Noticeably faster viewport, significantly faster rendering

Troubleshooting Hidden Parts

‚ö†Ô∏è Common Issues

Problem: Hidden body part shows in final render

Cause: Clothing transparent or camera angle reveals gap

Solution: Unhide that body part, or adjust clothing


Problem: Forgot what I hid

Solution: Check hierarchy panel‚Äîhidden items have closed eye icon


Problem: Want to quickly unhide everything

Solution: Go through hierarchy and click each closed eye icon. Or reload figure (loses clothing though)


Problem: Clothing looks weird after hiding body parts

Cause: Some clothing uses body parts as reference for positioning

Solution: Unhide the body part‚Äîthis is rare but can happen

Hiding vs. Transparency

Some people try to use transparency instead of hiding. Here's why that doesn't work as well:

Approach How It Works Performance Best Use
Hiding (visibility off) Geometry not rendered at all Excellent - geometry doesn't exist Completely covered body parts
Transparency Geometry rendered but invisible Poor - still calculates geometry When you need the volume but not the surface

Verdict: For completely covered body parts under clothing, always use hiding (visibility off), not transparency!

üéØ The Visibility Mindset: Think of hiding body parts like a movie set. The camera only films one angle‚Äîthe back of the set doesn't need to exist. In 3D, if the camera will never see it and it's completely covered, it doesn't need to render. Save that computing power for what matters!

üîß Troubleshooting Common Issues

Even with all this knowledge, things can still go wrong. Let's tackle the most common clothing problems and their solutions.

Issue 1: Poke-Through

‚ö†Ô∏è The Most Common Problem

Symptom: Body parts (breasts, shoulders, knees, elbows) poke through clothing

Causes:

  • Clothing not morphed to match figure's body shape
  • Extreme pose causing clothing to pull tight
  • Clothing designed for different body proportions
  • Multiple clothing layers fighting for same space

Solutions (try in order):

  1. Apply fit morphs: Bust adjust, smooth chest, expand, etc. (values: +0.2 to +0.5)
  2. Check for matching body morphs: If figure is "Muscular 0.8", apply "Muscular" morph to clothing too
  3. Adjust pose slightly: Sometimes small pose change eliminates problem
  4. Hide the body part: If completely covered, just hide it!
  5. Camera angle: Position camera to not show the problem (last resort)

Issue 2: Clothing Won't Conform

‚ö†Ô∏è The "Why Won't It Work?" Problem

Symptom: Figure ‚Üí Conform To is grayed out, or clothing doesn't wrap around figure

Causes & Solutions:

  • Wrong figure type:
    • Cause: Clothing designed for different figure (LaFemme clothing on Genesis 8 figure)
    • Solution: Get correct clothing for your figure type
  • Not actually conforming clothing:
    • Cause: Loaded a regular prop thinking it was clothing
    • Solution: Check library category‚Äîshould be in Clothing or Figures
  • No figure loaded:
    • Cause: Conforming needs a figure to conform TO
    • Solution: Load figure first, then clothing
  • Corrupted clothing file:
    • Cause: File installation error or download problem
    • Solution: Reinstall the clothing content

Issue 3: Clothing Explodes or Looks Distorted

‚ö†Ô∏è The "What Happened?!" Problem

Symptom: Clothing loads looking stretched, distorted, or pieces flying everywhere

Causes & Solutions:

  • Figure in extreme pose when clothing loaded:
    • Cause: Clothing tries to match extreme pose immediately, can't handle it
    • Solution: Put figure in zero pose (T-pose), load clothing, THEN pose figure
  • Scale mismatch:
    • Cause: Figure or clothing has wrong scale value
    • Solution: Check scale in parameters‚Äîshould be around 100%
  • Rigging problem:
    • Cause: Clothing file has broken rigging data
    • Solution: Reinstall clothing or contact content creator

Issue 4: Clothing Moves Separately from Figure

‚ö†Ô∏è The "It's Not Following" Problem

Symptom: Move figure's arm, clothing sleeve doesn't move with it

Cause: Clothing not properly conformed

Solutions:

  1. Check conforming status: Look in hierarchy‚Äîis clothing nested under figure?
  2. Reconform: Select clothing ‚Üí Figure ‚Üí Conform To ‚Üí Select figure
  3. If still doesn't work: Unconform, delete clothing, reload fresh, conform again

Issue 5: Multiple Clothing Items Interfering

‚ö†Ô∏è The "Too Crowded" Problem

Symptom: Layered clothing clipping through each other

Solutions:

  • Apply progressive expand morphs:
    • Inner layer: 0 or +0.1
    • Middle layer: +0.2 to +0.3
    • Outer layer: +0.4 to +0.6
  • Load in correct order: Inside out
  • Consider alternatives: Some clothing combinations just don't work well together
  • Hide inner layers: If not visible, hide them to reduce clipping

Issue 6: Smart Props in Wrong Location

‚ö†Ô∏è The "Glasses on the Floor" Problem

Symptom: Smart prop parents correctly but positioned wrong

Causes & Solutions:

  • Figure morphs:
    • Smart prop positioned for default figure
    • Your figure has different proportions (bigger head, etc.)
    • Solution: Manually adjust position and scale
  • Parented to wrong figure:
    • Multiple figures in scene
    • Solution: Object ‚Üí Change Parent ‚Üí Select correct figure's body part
  • Not truly a smart prop:
    • Regular prop that needs manual parenting
    • Solution: Manually parent to desired body part

Issue 7: Can't Find Clothing Morphs

‚ö†Ô∏è The "Where Are The Morphs?" Problem

Symptom: Opened parameters but can't find expected morphs

Solutions:

  • Make sure clothing is selected: Not figure‚Äîthe actual clothing item
  • Expand all categories: Morphs may be in Body ‚Üí Morphs or other nested locations
  • Check different body parts: Some morphs are under specific body parts (Chest, Hip, etc.)
  • Clothing may have limited morphs: Not all clothing comes with extensive morphs
  • Use search: Some parameter panels have search function

Issue 8: Clothing Looks Wrong After Saving/Loading

‚ö†Ô∏è The "It Looked Fine Before" Problem

Symptom: Saved scene, closed Poser, reopened‚Äîclothing is wrong

Possible causes:

  • Morphs not saved:
    • Some versions may not save all morph settings
    • Solution: Resave with different save options, or reapply morphs
  • Clothing library path changed:
    • Poser can't find clothing files
    • Solution: Check library paths in preferences
  • Conforming lost:
    • Clothing loaded but not conformed
    • Solution: Reconform all clothing items

The Systematic Troubleshooting Approach

‚úÖ When Things Go Wrong: Your Checklist

  1. Identify the specific problem (poke-through, wrong fit, won't conform, etc.)
  2. Check the obvious first
    • Is clothing selected when trying to use it?
    • Is it conformed? (check hierarchy)
    • Right clothing for this figure type?
  3. Try the simplest solution
    • Apply relevant morph
    • Adjust pose slightly
    • Reconform
  4. If that doesn't work, escalate
    • Delete and reload clothing
    • Check figure is at correct scale
    • Try different clothing item
  5. Last resort options
    • Hide problem body parts
    • Adjust camera to avoid showing problem
    • Contact content creator for support

üîß The Troubleshooter's Mantra: "90% of clothing problems are solved by morphs. 9% are solved by reconforming. The remaining 1% means you need different clothing." Start with morphs, work your way up, and you'll fix almost everything!

  4. Load any held items

    • Break symmetry
    • Weight distribution
  2. Check clothing with pose
    • Clothing should follow naturally
    • Look for new poke-through from bent limbs
  3. Adjust morphs for pose if needed
    • Bent arms may need elbow morphs
    • Bent legs may need knee morphs
  4. Adjust accessories for pose
    • Glasses stay put (good!)
    • Held items may need repositioning

    • Choose best view of character
    • Frame nicely
  2. Hide body parts

    • Fix any remaining issues
  4. Clean up hierarchy
    • All items named descriptively
    • Organization makes sense

    • Identify any issues not visible in viewport
  6. Save your work!
    • Descriptive filename
    • Include character style in name
