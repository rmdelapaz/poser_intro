Starting with a Fresh Scene

Before we load our first figure, let's make sure we're starting with a clean slate. If Poser opened with a figure already loaded (it often does), that's perfectly fine‚Äîbut let's learn how to create a new scene so you know how to start fresh anytime.

Creating a New Scene

To start a new, empty scene:

  1. Go to File > New in the menu bar
  2. Poser will ask if you want to save changes to your current scene (if there are any)
  3. Click No (we haven't made anything worth saving yet)
  4. Your workspace should now be empty‚Äîjust the grid floor and background

üí° Already Have a Figure Loaded?

If Poser loaded with a default figure (like Andy or a mannequin), you can either:

  • Keep it and skip to the next section (you're already ahead!)
  • Delete it to practice loading from scratch: Click the figure, press Delete
  • Start fresh with File > New
üé¨ Setting the Stage: Think of File > New as clearing the stage before a performance. You're giving yourself a blank canvas to work with, ready to add your actors (figures), set pieces (props), and lighting!

Loading Your First Figure

This is the exciting part! You're about to load your first 3D character. It's like opening a toy box and pulling out your first action figure‚Äîexcept this one you can pose, dress up, and photograph from any angle!

Opening the Library

First, make sure your Library palette is visible:

  1. Look for the Library panel (usually on the right side of the screen)
  2. If you don't see it, press Ctrl + L or go to Window > Libraries

Navigating to Figures

The Library palette has several categories at the top. We want the Figures category:

  1. Click on the Figures icon/button at the top of the Library
  2. You'll see a list of folders containing different types of figures
  3. Look for a folder called Poser 12 or Poser Default
  4. Inside, you should find figures like Andy, Jessie, or other base figures

Loading a Figure

To load a figure into your scene:

  1. Find a figure thumbnail you want to load (try Andy or Jessie‚Äîthe default Poser figures)
  2. Double-click the thumbnail
  3. Wait a moment... and tada! Your figure appears in the Document Window!

What You Should See

Once your figure loads, you should see:

  • A 3D figure standing in the center of your viewport
  • The figure standing on the grid floor
  • The figure in a default "neutral" pose (usually arms at sides, standing straight)
  • Good lighting showing the figure clearly

üéâ Congratulations!

You just loaded your first 3D figure! Take a moment to orbit the camera around it (remember: click and drag in empty space). Look at your figure from all angles. Pretty cool, right? This is your digital actor, ready to perform!

üé≠ Quick Analogy: Loading a figure is like calling an actor onto your stage. They show up in their default position, waiting for you (the director) to tell them how to stand, what to do, and where to look. You're in charge!

Troubleshooting: Figure Didn't Load?

If nothing appeared, try these fixes:

  • Check you're in the right category: Make sure you clicked Figures, not Props or Poses
  • Try a different figure: Some figures might have installation issues; try Andy or Jessie
  • Frame the scene: Press Shift + A to frame everything in view
  • Check for errors: Look for any error messages in the bottom of the Poser window

Understanding the Figure Hierarchy

Now that you have a figure loaded, let's understand how it's built. A Poser figure isn't just one solid object‚Äîit's made up of many individual body parts connected together in a hierarchy. Think of it like a skeleton with joints!

What is a Hierarchy?

A hierarchy is like a family tree or organizational chart. In Poser figures:

  • Parent parts control their child parts
  • Moving a parent automatically moves all its children
  • Children can move independently of their parents

Real-World Analogy: Your Own Body

Think about your own arm:

  • Shoulder is the parent of the upper arm
  • Upper arm is the parent of the forearm
  • Forearm is the parent of the hand
  • Hand is the parent of the fingers

When you lift your shoulder, your entire arm comes with it. But you can bend your elbow without moving your shoulder. That's how hierarchy works!

Viewing the Figure Hierarchy

To see the complete hierarchy of your figure:

  1. Look for the Hierarchy panel (usually on the left side)
  2. If you don't see it, go to Window > Hierarchy
  3. You'll see a tree-like list of all body parts

Major Body Parts and Their Children

Here's a simplified breakdown of a typical figure hierarchy:

Parent Part Child Parts What It Controls
Body (root) Hip, Chest, Head, Limbs The entire figure's position and rotation
Hip Abdomen, Pelvis, Thighs Lower body and stance
Chest Collars, Shoulders, Neck Upper body and torso twist
Head Eyes, Jaw, Tongue (if present) Head rotation and facial features
Shoulder Forearm, Hand, Fingers Entire arm from shoulder down
Hand Thumb, Index, Middle, Ring, Pinky All fingers of that hand
Thigh Shin, Foot, Toes Entire leg from hip down

üí° Pro Tip: Start from the Top

When posing, it's usually best to work from larger body parts down to smaller ones:

  1. Position the body/hip first (overall stance)
  2. Adjust chest and shoulders (upper body posture)
  3. Pose arms and legs (major limb positions)
  4. Fine-tune hands and fingers (details)
  5. Adjust head and face last (expressions)

This prevents you from having to redo work when parent parts move!

ü§ñ Think Like a Robot: Imagine your figure is a robot made of connected segments. Each segment (body part) can rotate at its joint, but it's attached to its parent segment. Understanding this connection is key to natural posing!

Selecting Body Parts

Before you can pose a body part, you need to select it. Poser gives you several ways to do this. Let's learn all the methods so you can use whichever feels most natural to you!

Method 1: Direct Clicking (Easiest for Beginners)

The most intuitive way to select a body part:

  1. Simply click directly on the body part in the Document Window
  2. The selected part will highlight (usually with a colored outline or glow)
  3. The name of the selected part appears somewhere on screen (often top or bottom)

‚úÖ What Happens When You Select

When a body part is selected, you'll notice:

  • The part highlights or glows in the viewport
  • The Properties Panel updates to show that part's parameters
  • The current selection name displays somewhere visible
  • Any tools or dials now affect only that selected part

Method 2: Using the Hierarchy Panel

For precise selection, especially of hard-to-click parts:

  1. Open the Hierarchy panel (Window > Hierarchy)
  2. Find the body part name in the list
  3. Click the part name
  4. That part is now selected!

Why use this method? Some body parts are small or hard to click (like individual fingers). The Hierarchy panel lets you select them by name without hunting for them in the 3D view.

Method 3: Using the Body Part Selection Menu

Poser also has a dropdown menu for body part selection:

  1. Look for a dropdown near the top of the interface (often says "Body" or the current part name)
  2. Click the dropdown to see a list of all body parts
  3. Select the part you want from the list

Method 4: Keyboard Shortcuts for Common Parts

Some body parts have quick selection shortcuts:

  • Selection cycling: Click the same area repeatedly to cycle through overlapping parts
  • Shift + Click: In some modes, adds to selection

‚ö†Ô∏è Can't Select a Part?

If clicking doesn't select the part you want:

  • Try clicking the exact center of the body part
  • Use the Hierarchy panel instead of clicking
  • Make sure you're in the right tool mode (not camera mode)
  • Zoom in closer to make smaller parts easier to click
  • Try a different camera angle where the part is more visible

Practice Exercise: The Selection Challenge

  2. Click the right shoulder

  6. Click the left thigh
  7. Click the left foot

Watch how the Properties Panel changes with each selection. Get comfortable with this‚Äîyou'll be selecting body parts constantly as you pose!

Using Parameter Dials to Pose

Now for the fun part‚Äîmaking your figure move! Parameter dials are your primary tool for posing. Think of them as the control knobs on your character puppet. Each dial controls a specific aspect of movement.

Understanding Rotation Parameters

The most important parameters for posing are the rotation dials. Most body parts have three rotation axes:

The Three Axes of Rotation

  • xRotate (Twist): Rotation around the X-axis - think of it as twisting or rolling
  • yRotate (Bend): Rotation around the Y-axis - the main bending motion
  • zRotate (Side-to-Side): Rotation around the Z-axis - swinging left or right

Twist/Roll] A --> C[yRotate
Bend Up/Down] A --> D[zRotate
Swing Side/Side] style A fill:#667eea,stroke:#333,stroke-width:2px,color:#fff

How to Use Parameter Dials

Once you've selected a body part, look at the Properties Panel. You'll see dials for that part:

  1. Find the parameter you want to adjust (like "yRotate" for the shoulder)
  2. Click and drag the dial left or right
  3. Watch the figure move in real-time as you drag
  4. Release when you like the position

Alternative Ways to Adjust Parameters

Besides dragging dials, you can:

  • Type a value: Click in the number field and type a specific degree value
  • Use arrow buttons: Click the small arrows next to the value for small increments
  • Double-click to reset: Double-click a dial to return it to zero/default

üí° Pro Tip: Fine Control

For precise adjustments:

  • Hold Shift while dragging for finer, slower movement
  • Hold Ctrl while dragging for larger, faster jumps
  • Use the number field for exact values when you need precision

Common Parameter Examples

Body Part Parameter What It Does Try This Value
Right Shoulder yRotate Raises/lowers the arm 90 (raises arm to horizontal)
Right Forearm yRotate Bends the elbow -90 (bends elbow 90 degrees)
Head yRotate Nods the head -20 (looks down slightly)
Head xRotate Tilts head side to side 15 (tilts right)
Head zRotate Turns head left/right 30 (turns to look right)
Left Thigh xRotate Lifts leg forward 45 (lifts leg forward)

Creating Your First Pose

Now that you understand how to select body parts and use parameter dials, let's create a complete pose from scratch. We'll start with something simple but natural: a relaxed standing pose.

The Principles of Natural Posing

Before we dive in, here are key principles that make poses look natural:

üé® Posing Principles

  • Avoid perfect symmetry: Real people don't stand perfectly balanced
  • Weight distribution matters: Shift weight to one leg for a natural stance
  • Subtle angles: Small rotations (5-15 degrees) add life
  • The line of action: The body should have a slight curve or flow
  • Opposing curves: If hip tilts one way, shoulders often tilt the opposite way

Step-by-Step: Creating a Relaxed Standing Pose

Let's create a natural, casual standing pose. Follow along with your figure!

Step 1: Position the Hip (Weight Distribution)

  1. Select the Hip
  2. Set zRotate to about 5 (tilts hip slightly to one side)
  3. This makes it look like weight is shifted to one leg

Step 2: Counter with the Chest

  1. Select the Chest
  2. Set zRotate to about -3 (tilts slightly opposite direction)
  3. This creates a natural counterbalance

Step 3: Add a Head Tilt

  1. Select the Head
  2. Set xRotate to about 5 (slight tilt)
  3. Set zRotate to about 8 (turn slightly to one side)
  4. This adds personality and breaks perfect symmetry

Step 4: Relax the Arms

  1. Select Right Shoulder
  2. Set yRotate to about -10 (arm slightly forward)
  3. Set zRotate to about 8 (arm slightly away from body)
  4. Repeat similar values for Left Shoulder but slightly different (asymmetry!)

Step 5: Bend the Elbows Slightly

  1. Select Right Forearm
  2. Set yRotate to about -15 (slight bend)
  3. Repeat for Left Forearm with a slightly different value like -12

Step 6: Adjust the Legs for Natural Stance

  1. Select Left Thigh (the weight-bearing leg)
  2. Keep it mostly straight, maybe xRotate: -5
  3. Select Right Thigh (the relaxed leg)
  4. Set xRotate to about 10 and zRotate to about 8 (leg slightly bent and out)

‚ö†Ô∏è Don't Worry About Perfection!

Your first pose doesn't need to be perfect. The goal is to:

  • Get comfortable selecting body parts
  • Practice using parameter dials
  • See how small changes affect the overall pose
  • Build confidence in the posing process

Professional poses come with practice. For now, experiment and have fun!

üé≠ The Magic of Small Adjustments: Notice how just a few degree changes (5-15 degrees) make a huge difference? That's the secret to natural poses. You're not making big, dramatic rotations‚Äîyou're making subtle, thoughtful adjustments that add up to something alive and believable!

Framing Your Figure

Now that you've posed your figure, let's make sure we're viewing it from the best angle. Just like a photographer, you want to frame your subject well!

Using Frame Selected

The quickest way to center your figure in view:

  1. Make sure your figure (or any part of it) is selected
  2. Press Shift + F
  3. The camera automatically moves to frame the selected part perfectly

Framing the Entire Figure

To see your whole figure in the frame:

  1. Select the Body (the root of the figure)
  2. Press Shift + A (Frame All)
  3. The camera pulls back to show the complete figure

Manual Camera Adjustment

For more control over your view:

  • Orbit: Click and drag in empty space to rotate around the figure
  • Zoom: Scroll wheel to move closer or further
  • Pan: Alt + Click and drag to slide the view

üí° Finding Your Best Angle

Try viewing your pose from these classic angles:

  • Front view: See the pose head-on
  • 3/4 view: Orbit about 45 degrees‚Äîthis is often the most flattering
  • Side view: Check the pose in profile
  • Slightly above: Look down at a slight angle (like you're taller than the figure)

Professional tip: The 3/4 view from slightly above is a classic for a reason‚Äîit's almost always flattering!

Saving Your Work

You've created your first pose‚Äîcongratulations! Now let's make sure you don't lose it. Saving in Poser is straightforward, but let's cover all the important points.

Saving Your Scene File

To save your current scene with your posed figure:

  1. Go to File > Save As (for your first save)
  2. Choose a location on your computer (create a "Poser Projects" folder if you haven't)
  3. Give your file a descriptive name like "my_first_pose"
  4. Make sure the file type is .pz3 (Poser Scene File)
  5. Click Save

‚úÖ What Gets Saved in a Scene File

Your .pz3 scene file contains:

  • All figures in your scene and their poses
  • All props and their positions
  • Camera positions and settings
  • Lighting setup
  • Material settings
  • Basically, everything about your scene!

Quick Save

After your initial save, use the quick save:

  • Press Ctrl + S
  • Or go to File > Save
  • This updates your existing file without prompting for a name

‚ö†Ô∏è Save Often!

Get into the habit of saving regularly:

  • Save when you start working (File > Save As)
  • Save after making significant changes (Ctrl + S)
  • Save before trying something experimental
  • Save before rendering

There's no autosave in Poser, so manual saving is crucial!

Loading a Saved Scene

To open a previously saved scene:

  1. Go to File > Open
  2. Navigate to your saved .pz3 file
  3. Click Open
  4. Your scene loads exactly as you saved it!
üíæ The Golden Rule: Save early, save often! Professional artists save multiple versions of their work (my_pose_v1, my_pose_v2, etc.) so they can always go back if they need to. You never know when you'll want to return to an earlier version!

Project: Your First Standing Pose

It's time to put everything together! This project will help you practice all the skills you've learned in this lesson.

üéØ Project Brief

Create a natural, relaxed standing pose for your figure. The pose should look like someone casually waiting for a friend or browsing in a store‚Äînatural and unstudied.

Requirements:

  • Use weight shift (hip tilt)
  • Break symmetry (arms and legs different)
  • Add subtle head positioning
  • Make it look relaxed and natural
  • Save your scene with a descriptive name

Step-by-Step Project Guide

Phase 1: Foundation (5 minutes)

  1. Start with a fresh figure (load Andy or Jessie)
  2. Establish weight distribution by tilting the hip
  3. Counter-tilt the chest
  4. Frame your figure with Shift + A

Phase 2: Upper Body (10 minutes)

  1. Position and tilt the head naturally
  2. Adjust both shoulders (remember: make them different!)
  3. Add subtle elbow bends
  4. Orbit the camera to check from different angles

Phase 3: Lower Body (10 minutes)

  1. Adjust the weight-bearing leg (keep mostly straight)
  2. Relax the other leg (slight bend and rotation)
  3. Check feet positions (they should look grounded)

Phase 4: Refinement (10 minutes)

  1. Orbit around your figure, checking all angles
  2. Make small adjustments to anything that looks stiff
  3. Add personality with hand positions
  4. Find your best camera angle

Phase 5: Save Your Work

  1. Save your scene: File > Save As
  2. Name it something like "first_standing_pose_v1.pz3"
  3. Make note of what you learned

üé® Creative Variations to Try

Once you complete the basic pose, experiment with variations:

  • Confident stance: Broader stance, shoulders back, head up
  • Shy pose: Arms closer to body, head tilted down, weight on one leg
  • Relaxed casual: One hand in pocket position, head tilted, easy stance
  • Curious look: Weight forward, head turned, one arm reaching

Save each variation as a new file (v2, v3, etc.)

Self-Assessment Checklist

Use this checklist to evaluate your pose:

‚úÖ Pose Quality Checklist

  • ‚ñ° Weight appears shifted to one side
  • ‚ñ° Pose is not perfectly symmetrical
  • ‚ñ° Body has subtle curves (not stiff and straight)
  • ‚ñ° Head has personality (tilted or turned)

  • ‚ñ° Elbows have slight bends (not locked straight)
  • ‚ñ° Legs look natural (one relaxed, one bearing weight)
  • ‚ñ° Overall pose looks comfortable and sustainable
  • ‚ñ° Viewed from multiple angles and looks good
  • ‚ñ° Scene saved with descriptive filename
