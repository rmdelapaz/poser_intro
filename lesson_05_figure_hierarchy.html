What is Figure Hierarchy?

Imagine you're building a puppet. You can't just attach strings to random spots‚Äîyou need to understand how the parts connect. The shoulder connects to the arm, the arm to the forearm, the forearm to the hand. Each connection matters. That's exactly what figure hierarchy is in Poser: the organizational structure that defines how body parts relate to and control each other.

The Skeleton Metaphor

Think of your own skeleton. Your bones aren't just floating around randomly‚Äîthey're connected in a specific order:

  • Your spine connects to your skull
  • Your spine connects to your shoulders
  • Your shoulders connect to your upper arms
  • Your upper arms connect to your forearms
  • And so on...

In Poser, figures are built the same way. Each body part is a "bone" in the digital skeleton, and these bones are arranged in a hierarchy‚Äîa family tree of body parts!

üí° Why Hierarchy Matters

Understanding hierarchy helps you:

  • Pose more efficiently: Adjust parents to move multiple children at once
  • Avoid frustration: Understand why body parts move unexpectedly
  • Create natural movement: Work with the body's natural structure
  • Troubleshoot problems: Know where to look when something isn't working
  • Work faster: Select and adjust the right parts on the first try
üé≠ The Puppet Master Insight: Professional puppeteers don't pull random strings‚Äîthey know exactly which string controls which part. Similarly, understanding hierarchy transforms you from someone randomly clicking body parts to a master who knows exactly which part to adjust for the perfect pose!

Parent-Child Relationships

This is the core concept that makes everything else make sense. In Poser's hierarchy, every body part (except the root) has a parent, and most parts have one or more children.

The Golden Rules of Parent-Child

Rule #1: Children Follow Parents

When you move a parent, ALL of its children move with it automatically.

Example: Rotate the shoulder, and the entire arm (forearm, hand, fingers) rotates with it.

Rule #2: Children Are Independent

When you move a child, the parent stays put. Children can move freely without affecting their parents.

Example: Bend the forearm at the elbow, and the shoulder doesn't move at all.

Rule #3: Grandchildren Follow Too

The relationship cascades down. Move a parent, and children, grandchildren, and great-grandchildren all follow.

Example: Rotate the shoulder, and the forearm, hand, AND all five fingers move together as one unit.

Real-World Analogy: The Mobile

Imagine a hanging mobile (like a baby's crib mobile):

  • The ceiling hook is the root parent
  • Move the ceiling hook, and everything below moves
  • The main rod hangs from the hook (child of hook)
  • The smaller rods hang from the main rod (grandchildren)
  • The shapes hang from the smaller rods (great-grandchildren)

Sway the ceiling hook, and every single piece moves. But you can spin one small shape without affecting anything above it!

üí™ The Arm-Lifting Test: Try this with your own arm right now. Lift your shoulder‚Äîyour whole arm lifts. Now keep your shoulder still and bend your elbow‚Äîonly your forearm moves. Now keep your arm still and curl your fingers‚Äîonly your hand changes. Your body IS a hierarchy. Poser just digitizes it!

‚ö†Ô∏è Common Beginner Mistake

The Problem: "I moved the hand and the whole arm moved! Why?"

The Reality: You probably selected the shoulder or forearm by mistake, not the hand. When you think you're moving a child but the parent is actually selected, the whole chain follows.

The Fix: Always double-check which body part is selected before adjusting parameters. The selected part's name should be clearly displayed!

The Human Figure Structure

Let's map out the complete hierarchy of a typical Poser figure. This is your reference guide‚Äîkeep it handy!

The Root: Body or Hip

Every Poser figure has a root body part‚Äîtypically called "Body" or "Hip." This is the ultimate parent. Move the root, and the ENTIRE figure moves as one unit.

üå≥ Think of It Like a Tree

The root is like a tree trunk. All branches (body parts) connect to it eventually. Move the trunk, and every branch, twig, and leaf moves with it!

The Major Branches

From the root, major body sections branch out:

üí° Naming Conventions

Poser uses consistent naming:

  • Left/Right: Always from the figure's perspective, not yours!
  • Shoulder vs. Forearm: "Shoulder" is upper arm, "Forearm" is lower arm (confusing but standard)
  • Shin: Lower leg (some figures call it "Calf")
  • Thigh: Upper leg

Using the Hierarchy Panel

The Hierarchy Panel is your visual map of the figure's structure. It shows every body part in a tree view, making it easy to see parent-child relationships at a glance.

Opening the Hierarchy Panel

If you don't see the Hierarchy Panel:

  1. Go to Window > Hierarchy
  2. The panel appears (usually on the left side)
  3. You'll see a tree structure with expandable/collapsible branches

Reading the Hierarchy Panel

The panel shows body parts in a tree structure:

  • Indentation shows relationships: Indented items are children
  • Plus/minus icons: Expand or collapse branches
  • Highlight indicates selection: Selected part is highlighted
  • Top to bottom: Reads from parent down to children

Using the Panel to Select Parts

The Hierarchy Panel is incredibly useful for selection:

  1. Click any body part name in the panel
  2. That part becomes selected in the 3D view
  3. The Properties Panel updates to show that part's parameters

Why this is useful:

  • Select small parts easily (individual finger segments)
  • No hunting around the 3D view clicking on things
  • See at a glance what you have selected
  • Understand parent-child relationships visually

‚úÖ Pro Workflow: Dual Selection

Professionals often use both methods together:

  1. Click in 3D view for major body parts (fast and intuitive)
  2. Use Hierarchy Panel for precise selection (fingers, small parts)
  3. Glance at panel to verify what's selected
  4. Expand/collapse branches to focus on relevant areas

Navigating the Tree

Tips for efficient hierarchy navigation:

  • Collapse unused branches: Click minus sign to hide arm structure while working on legs
  • Expand relevant sections: Open the hand branch when posing fingers
  • Use the scroll bar: Long lists can be scrolled
  • Right-click options: Some panels offer right-click menus with shortcuts

Understanding the Chain Reaction

Now that you understand the structure, let's see it in action. When you move a parent, you create a "chain reaction" that flows down the hierarchy. Understanding this helps you predict and control figure behavior.

The Domino Effect

Think of hierarchy like dominoes lined up:

  • Knock over the first domino (move parent) ‚Üí all the others fall (children follow)
  • Remove a domino from the middle (move child) ‚Üí only dominoes after it fall (descendants follow)
  • The dominoes before it (parents) stay standing

Practical Examples of Chain Reactions

Example 1: Raising the Arm

Action: Rotate the right shoulder (yRotate: 90)

Result:

  • ‚úÖ Shoulder rotates up
  • ‚úÖ Forearm follows (maintains angle relative to shoulder)
  • ‚úÖ Hand follows
  • ‚úÖ All five fingers follow
  • ‚úÖ Everything maintains its bent/straight state
  • ‚ùå Chest stays put (parent doesn't move)

Why: Shoulder is parent to everything else in the arm

Example 2: Bending the Elbow

Action: Rotate the right forearm (yRotate: -90)

Result:

  • ‚úÖ Forearm bends at elbow
  • ‚úÖ Hand follows (keeps same orientation to forearm)
  • ‚úÖ All fingers follow
  • ‚ùå Shoulder stays put (parent)
  • ‚ùå Chest stays put (grandparent)

Why: Forearm is parent to hand and fingers only

Example 3: Curling a Finger

Action: Rotate the right index finger (first segment)

Result:

  • ‚úÖ Index finger curls
  • ‚úÖ Remaining segments of index finger follow
  • ‚ùå Other fingers stay put (siblings, not children)
  • ‚ùå Hand stays put (parent)
  • ‚ùå Arm stays put (grandparent)

Why: Each finger is independent; only its own segments are children

Example 4: Twisting the Chest

Action: Rotate the chest (yRotate: 30)

Result:

  • ‚úÖ Chest rotates
  • ‚úÖ Both collars follow
  • ‚úÖ Both complete arms follow
  • ‚úÖ Neck and head follow
  • ‚ùå Hip/pelvis stays put (parent)
  • ‚ùå Legs stay put (different branch)

Why: Chest is parent to upper body, but child of hip

Predicting Movement

Before you move any body part, ask yourself:

  1. What am I selecting? (Verify in Hierarchy Panel)
  2. What are its children? (They will follow)
  3. What is its parent? (It will stay put)
  4. Is this the part I really want to move?

‚ö†Ô∏è Common Chain Reaction Confusion

Problem: "I wanted to bend the elbow, but the whole arm moved!"

Cause: You selected and rotated the shoulder (parent) instead of the forearm (child)

Solution: Click the forearm specifically, or use the Hierarchy Panel to select "Right Forearm"

üéÆ Game-ify Your Learning: Challenge yourself! Pick a body part and, BEFORE moving it, predict out loud which parts will follow and which will stay put. Then move it and check if you were right. This mental exercise will make you a posing master fast!

Inverse Kinematics (IK) Basics

So far, we've talked about Forward Kinematics (FK)‚Äîmove a parent and children follow. But Poser also supports Inverse Kinematics (IK), which works backwards: move a child (like a hand) and the parents (arm) adjust automatically to reach it!

FK vs. IK: The Key Difference

Forward Kinematics (FK) - The Normal Way

How it works: You adjust parents, and children follow

Example: Rotate the shoulder ‚Üí forearm follows ‚Üí hand follows

Best for: Natural arm swings, gestures, most posing

Think of it as: "Move the shoulder to where I want the hand"

Inverse Kinematics (IK) - The Magic Way

How it works: You move a child (goal), and parents figure out how to reach it

Example: Drag the hand to a spot ‚Üí shoulder and forearm automatically bend to reach it

Best for: Reaching for objects, planting feet on ground, precise positioning

Think of it as: "Put the hand HERE, figure out the rest"

Real-World IK Example

Imagine picking up a coffee cup:

  • With FK: You'd manually rotate shoulder, then forearm, then fine-tune both until hand reaches cup (tedious!)
  • With IK: You'd move the hand to the cup, and the arm automatically bends to reach it (magic!)

When IK is Enabled in Poser

Most Poser figures have IK turned on by default for:

  • Arms: IK chains from shoulder to hand
  • Legs: IK chains from hip to foot

You can toggle IK on/off in figure properties or through special controls (we'll cover this more in depth in later lessons).

Quick IK Tips for Now

  • Use FK for most posing: It's more intuitive and gives you direct control
  • Use IK for specific goals: "Put foot here," "reach for that object"
  • IK can be tricky: Sometimes produces unexpected bends
  • Learn FK first: Master the basics before diving deep into IK

üí° IK Explained with a Robot Arm

Imagine a robot arm picking up a ball:

FK approach: You program each joint angle: "Shoulder 45¬∞, elbow 60¬∞, wrist 20¬∞"

IK approach: You just say "Hand needs to be at these X, Y, Z coordinates" and the robot calculates the joint angles automatically!

Same idea in Poser. IK is powerful but not always necessary!

üéØ For This Lesson: Don't worry too much about IK yet. Understanding hierarchy and FK is your foundation. We'll dive deep into IK techniques in later lessons when you're comfortable with the basics!

Working with Symmetry and Mirroring

The human body is roughly symmetrical‚Äîleft arm mirrors right arm, left leg mirrors right leg. Poser has tools to help you take advantage of this symmetry, saving you tons of time!

Why Symmetry Matters

When posing:

  • You often want both arms doing similar things (just mirrored)
  • Both legs typically mirror each other in stance
  • Adjusting one side then manually replicating on the other is tedious

Symmetry tools let you: Pose one side, then mirror it to the other side automatically!

Understanding Left vs. Right

Important: In Poser, "left" and "right" are ALWAYS from the figure's perspective, not yours!

‚ö†Ô∏è The Left/Right Confusion

When you're looking at a figure facing you:

  • The arm on YOUR left is the figure's RIGHT arm
  • The arm on YOUR right is the figure's LEFT arm

Remember: It's like shaking hands with someone. Your right hand shakes their right hand!

Using Symmetry in Posing

Poser offers symmetry options in various ways (exact methods vary by version):

Common Symmetry Workflows

  1. Pose one side completely (e.g., the right arm)
  2. Select the posed parts you want to mirror
  3. Use a symmetry command (often in Edit menu or right-click menu)
  4. Choose "Copy Right to Left" (or vice versa)
  5. The pose mirrors automatically to the other side!

When to Use Symmetry

Situation Use Symmetry? Why
Standing at attention ‚úÖ Yes Both arms should match exactly
Raising both hands ‚úÖ Yes Mirror action saves time
Natural standing pose ‚ùå No Asymmetry looks more natural
Action pose (punching) ‚ùå No Opposite sides do different things
Walking pose ‚ùå No Legs alternate, not mirror
Holding a ball with both hands ‚úÖ Yes (with adjustments) Start with mirror, then fine-tune

Breaking Symmetry for Realism

Remember from earlier lessons: perfectly symmetrical poses look stiff and unnatural. Even when you use symmetry tools, consider:

  • Start with symmetry for efficiency
  • Then break it slightly for realism
  • Make one arm slightly higher, one leg slightly bent differently
  • Small asymmetries create life and naturalness

‚úÖ Smart Symmetry Workflow

  1. Pose one side (e.g., right arm)
  2. Mirror to other side using symmetry tools
  3. Check the result
  4. Make small adjustments to break perfect symmetry
  5. Add 3-5 degrees difference here and there
  6. Result: Fast posing that still looks natural!
üé≠ The Symmetry Paradox: Human bodies are symmetric in structure but asymmetric in motion. Use symmetry tools to save time on structure, then add asymmetry in motion to create life. That's the pro secret!

Practical Hierarchy Tips

Let's wrap up with practical tips that will make you a hierarchy master in your daily posing work!

The Top-Down Posing Strategy

Work from large body parts down to small ones:

  1. Position the body/hip first (overall stance and weight)
  2. Adjust chest and abdomen (torso twist and posture)
  3. Position major limbs (shoulders, thighs)
  4. Bend joints (elbows, knees)
  5. Refine extremities (hands, feet)
  6. Fine-tune details (fingers, head tilt)

Why this order? If you pose fingers first then move the shoulder, you'll have to redo the finger work. Work parent to child!

The Selection Verification Habit

Before adjusting any parameter, always verify:

  • ‚úÖ Look at the Hierarchy Panel - what's highlighted?
  • ‚úÖ Check the Properties Panel - what's the part name?
  • ‚úÖ Look for the highlight in the 3D view

This three-second check saves minutes of frustration!

Quick Reference: Common Mistakes

Problem Cause Solution
Whole arm moved, wanted elbow only Selected shoulder, not forearm Click forearm specifically
Can't get hand to right position Fighting the hierarchy chain Adjust shoulder first, then forearm
Legs moved when twisting torso Selected hip (parent of legs) Select chest or abdomen instead
One finger moved, others followed Selected hand (parent of all fingers) Click the specific finger
Can't select small body part Clicking inaccurately in 3D view Use Hierarchy Panel to select by name

Hierarchy Cheat Sheet

üìù Quick Reference

Move these to affect large areas:

  • Body: Entire figure
  • Hip: Stance, leg positioning
  • Chest: Upper body, both arms
  • Shoulder: Entire arm
  • Thigh: Entire leg

Move these for specific joints:

  • Forearm: Elbow bend
  • Hand: Wrist rotation
  • Shin: Knee bend
  • Foot: Ankle

Move these for details:

  • Individual fingers: Hand gestures
  • Head: Look direction
  • Eyes: Gaze direction

Project: Hierarchy Explorer

Time to cement your understanding with hands-on exploration!

Step-by-Step Project Guide

  1. Load your favorite base figure (Andy, Jessie, etc.)
  2. Open the Hierarchy Panel (Window > Hierarchy)
  3. Expand all branches so you can see the complete tree
  4. Position your camera for a good side view

Part 2: Exploration (20 minutes)

For each body part below, do this test:

  1. Click the body part name in Hierarchy Panel
  2. Rotate it (pick any rotation parameter, use about 30-45 degrees)
  3. Observe: What moved? What stayed put?
  4. Press Ctrl+Z to undo
  5. Write down or mentally note the relationships

Test these body parts:

  • Body (root)
  • Hip
  • Chest
  • Right Shoulder
  • Right Forearm
  • Right Hand
  • Right Index Finger
  • Right Thigh
  • Right Shin
  • Head

Part 3: Hierarchy Challenge Pose (15 minutes)

Create a pose that demonstrates you understand hierarchy by following these specific instructions:

  1. Twist the chest 30 degrees (both arms should follow)
  2. Raise the right shoulder (whole right arm should lift)
  3. Bend the right forearm -90 degrees (hand should follow, shoulder should stay)
  4. Rotate the right hand (fingers should follow, arm should stay)
  5. Lift the left thigh (whole left leg should lift)
  6. Bend the left shin (foot should bend, thigh should stay)
  7. Tilt the head (head only, body should stay)

If you completed these steps correctly, you should have a figure with:

  • Twisted torso
  • Right arm raised with bent elbow
  • Left leg raised with bent knee
  • Tilted head

Part 4: Reflection (5 minutes)

Answer these questions:

  1. Which body part is the parent of the most children?
  2. What happens when you rotate the Body (root)?
  3. How many parts move when you rotate a shoulder?
  4. What's the difference between rotating chest vs. hip?
  5. Did anything surprise you about the hierarchy structure?
