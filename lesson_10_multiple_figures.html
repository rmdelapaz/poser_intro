Why Multiple Figures Matter

Humans are social creatures. We exist in relationship to others. A lone character can be contemplative or isolated, but add a second character and suddenly you have relationship, conflict, love, tension, friendship‚Äîthe building blocks of every story!

üí° The Storytelling Power of "And"

Watch how meaning changes:

  • One character sitting: Person is resting
  • Two characters sitting: They're having a conversation
  • One character walking: Person is going somewhere
  • Two characters walking: They're traveling together (relationship!)
  • One character gesturing: Unclear purpose
  • Two characters, one gesturing: Communication is happening

Adding a second character doesn't double the story possibilities‚Äîit multiplies them exponentially!

What Multiple Figures Add

Relationship Dynamics

The way characters relate reveals story:

  • Distance between them: Close = intimacy, Far = distance/conflict
  • Body orientation: Facing each other = engagement, Turned away = rejection
  • Eye contact: Direct = connection, Avoiding = discomfort
  • Pose mirroring: Similar poses = agreement, Opposite = conflict
  • Height differences: Who's dominant? Who's submissive?

Visual Interest and Complexity

  • More elements create richer compositions
  • Different poses add visual variety
  • Multiple focal points guide viewer's eye
  • Creates natural visual triangles and shapes

Narrative Depth

  • Conflict: Two opposing characters create tension
  • Alliance: Characters together suggest shared purpose
  • Hierarchy: Who leads? Who follows?
  • Emotion multiplier: Happy + sad = bittersweet scene

Scale and Context

  • Second figure provides size reference
  • Shows realistic proportions
  • Makes scene feel more populated and alive

The Challenge

Multiple figures also mean:

  • More complex technical management
  • Potential for intersecting geometry (figures clipping through each other)
  • More demanding on computer resources
  • Requires careful composition to avoid chaos
  • Every figure needs proper posing and expression

But the reward is worth it! Master multi-figure scenes and your storytelling capabilities expand dramatically.

üé¨ Director's Philosophy: "One actor is a portrait. Two actors is a scene. Three actors is a story. Each additional character multiplies the dramatic possibilities‚Äîuse that power wisely!"

Loading and Managing Multiple Figures

Before we dive into creative aspects, let's handle the technical foundation of working with multiple figures.

Loading Additional Figures

Method 1: Load from Library (Standard)

  1. Your first figure is already in scene
  2. Go to Library > Figures
  3. Navigate to desired figure
  4. Double-click to load
  5. Second figure loads at origin (0,0,0)
  6. Important: Likely overlapping first figure!
  7. Immediately move second figure away

Method 2: Duplicate Existing Figure

  1. Select existing figure (click on any part)
  2. Edit > Duplicate (or Ctrl+D / Cmd+D)
  3. Duplicate creates exact copy with same pose
  4. Appears at same location (overlapping)
  5. Move immediately
  6. Benefit: Same figure type, can modify pose

Naming and Organization

Critical for multi-figure scenes!

‚úÖ Naming Best Practices

Poser typically names figures sequentially:

  • Figure 1, Figure 2, Figure 3 (default names)
  • Rename immediately to:
    • Hero, Villain, Sidekick (role-based)
    • John, Mary, Bob (character names)
    • MainChar, SupportChar1, Background1 (function-based)
  • How to rename: Select figure, find name field in properties or hierarchy

Managing Figure Hierarchy

With multiple figures, your hierarchy panel becomes essential:

Element Organization Strategy
Main character Top of figure list, descriptive name
Supporting characters Below main, clearly differentiated names
Background figures Group together, number if many (Crowd01, Crowd02)
Props parented to figures Nested under respective figure

Selection Management

With multiple overlapping figures, selection becomes tricky:

Selection Techniques

  • Click in viewport: Selects visible figure part
  • Click again (if multiple overlap): Cycles through overlapping elements
  • Use hierarchy panel: Most reliable‚Äîclick figure name
  • Frame selected: Press F to center camera on selected figure
  • Isolate in viewport: Hide other figures temporarily

Positioning New Figures

Workflow for adding each new figure:

  1. Load figure (double-click in library)
  2. Immediately select (should be auto-selected)
  3. Move away from origin using translate tool
    • xTran: Move left/right (e.g., +200 or -200)
    • zTran: Move forward/back (e.g., +100 or -100)
  4. Rename figure descriptively
  5. Rough pose if desired
  6. Repeat for additional figures

‚ö†Ô∏è Common Loading Issues

Problem: Can't see new figure / Lost figure

Cause: Inside another figure or way off camera

Solution: Select in hierarchy, press F to frame, or use top/side camera view


Problem: Figures clipping through each other

Cause: Loaded at same location

Solution: Move immediately after loading, use translate tool


Problem: Scene getting slow with multiple figures

Cause: More geometry = more processing

Solution: Lower preview quality, hide figures not currently working on, use simpler figures for background

Performance Considerations

Multiple figures impact performance:

Number of Figures Performance Impact Strategy
2-3 figures Minimal Work normally
4-6 figures Moderate Lower preview quality, hide when not needed
7-10 figures Significant Use simpler figures for background, work in stages
10+ figures (crowd) Heavy Consider duplicating posed figures, use low-poly models for distant characters
üíª Performance Tip: When building complex scenes, pose and refine your main 2-3 characters first. Add background figures last, after main storytelling elements are perfect. This keeps your workflow smooth!

Creating Natural Interactions

The magic of multi-figure scenes is making characters feel like they're actually interacting with each other, not just standing near each other!

The Interaction Checklist

For any two interacting characters, consider:

1. Body Orientation

Question: Are they engaged with each other or not?

  • Facing each other (¬±30¬∞): Engaged conversation, confrontation
  • 90¬∞ angle: Casual interaction, not fully engaged
  • Back-to-back: Tension, guarding, or casual companionship
  • Same direction: Shared focus on something else, united purpose

2. Distance Between Characters

Question: What's their relationship?

  • Intimate distance (0-18 inches): Lovers, very close friends, or invading personal space (conflict)
  • Personal distance (1.5-4 feet): Friends, friendly conversation
  • Social distance (4-12 feet): Acquaintances, formal situations
  • Public distance (12+ feet): Public speaking, strangers, avoidance

3. Eye Contact and Gaze

Question: Are they looking at each other?

  • Mutual gaze: Direct communication, attraction, or confrontation
  • One looking, one not: Asymmetric attention (one interested, one distracted)
  • Both looking elsewhere: Shared focus on external object/event
  • Avoiding eye contact: Discomfort, shame, disinterest

We'll cover this in depth in the next section!

4. Gesture and Body Language Coordination

Question: Do their poses relate to each other?

  • Mirrored poses: Agreement, rapport, harmony
  • Complementary poses: One reaching, one receiving
  • Contrasting poses: Disagreement, conflict, different emotions
  • Reactive poses: One gestures, other reacts visibly

Interaction Types and How to Pose Them

Conversation

Setup:

  • Characters facing each other (30-45¬∞ offset)
  • Personal distance (2-4 feet)
  • Both looking at each other
  • Open body language
  • One gesturing (speaking), other listening pose

Variations: Animated discussion (both gesturing), serious talk (both still), casual chat (one relaxed)

Confrontation

Setup:

  • Characters facing directly (or very close to it)
  • Intimate to personal distance (close = more aggressive)
  • Direct eye contact (intense)
  • Tense body language
  • Possibly one pointing, other defensive

Key: Tension visible in poses‚Äîclenched fists, tight stances

Romantic/Intimate

Setup:

  • Very close (intimate distance)
  • Bodies angled toward each other
  • Soft eye contact or gazing at each other
  • Touching (hands held, embrace, etc.)
  • Relaxed, open postures

Key: Closeness and gentle touching convey intimacy

Collaborative/Working Together

Setup:

  • Both facing same direction OR toward shared object
  • Personal to social distance
  • Looking at task/object, not each other
  • Complementary poses (one holding, one reaching)

Key: Unified focus on external goal

Teaching/Showing

Setup:

  • Teacher gesturing/pointing at object or demonstrating
  • Student watching teacher or object
  • Personal distance
  • One active, one receptive

Key: Clear roles‚Äîone demonstrating, one learning

The Action-Reaction Principle

One of the most powerful multi-figure techniques:

üí° For Every Action, Show a Reaction

The Rule: If one character does something, the other(s) should visibly react!

Examples:

  • One character points ‚Üí Other looks in that direction
  • One character speaks animatedly ‚Üí Other shows interest or skepticism
  • One character reaches toward other ‚Üí Other leans back or forward
  • One character looks aggressive ‚Üí Other defensive or aggressive back

This action-reaction dynamic makes interactions feel REAL!

üé≠ Acting Director's Secret: "When directing actors, I tell them to always LISTEN and REACT, even when they're not speaking. The same applies to 3D characters‚Äîevery figure should be actively participating in the scene, even if just observing!"

Eye Contact and Gaze Direction

Eye contact is perhaps THE single most powerful tool for creating believable character interactions. Get this right, and your scenes come alive!

Why Gaze Direction Matters

Humans are hardwired to follow eye gaze. When we see someone looking at something, we instinctively look there too. In multi-figure scenes:

  • Gaze creates invisible connection lines between characters
  • Reveals who's paying attention to whom
  • Shows power dynamics (who looks at whom)
  • Guides viewer's eye through the composition

Setting Up Eye Contact in Poser

Method 1: Manual Eye Positioning

  1. Select eye: Click on character's eye (left or right)
  2. Find rotation parameters: Usually xRotate (up/down) and yRotate (left/right)
  3. Adjust yRotate: Turn eye toward other character
    • Positive values = look right
    • Negative values = look left
    • Small adjustments (5-15¬∞) often sufficient
  4. Adjust xRotate: Look up or down if characters at different heights
  5. Repeat for other eye: Should match or be very close

Method 2: Point At (If Available)

Some Poser versions have "Point At" functionality:

  1. Select character's head or eyes
  2. Look for "Point At" parameter or tool
  3. Target second character's head
  4. Eyes automatically track that character
  5. Can adjust intensity of tracking

Benefit: If you move second character, eyes follow automatically!

Types of Eye Contact

Type Setup Meaning
Mutual Direct Gaze Both looking at each other's eyes Intense connection, confrontation, or attraction
One-Way Gaze A looks at B, B looks elsewhere A interested, B distracted/disinterested
Shared Focus Both looking at same external object Collaboration, shared interest
Avoidant Both looking away from each other Tension, awkwardness, or casual disregard
Glancing Looking at but not directly at eyes Shyness, politeness, or checking status

The Eye Contact - Emotion Matrix

Emotion/Situation Eye Contact Pattern
Love/Attraction Prolonged mutual gaze, soft focus
Anger/Confrontation Direct, intense, unblinking mutual stare
Conversation (speaker) Looking at listener, occasional glances away
Conversation (listener) Mostly looking at speaker
Shame/Guilt Looking down or away, avoiding eye contact
Fear/Submission Brief glances at threat, mostly avoidance
Dominance Direct stare at subordinate
Suspicion Narrowed eyes, intense focus, slight side-eye

Common Gaze Mistakes

‚ö†Ô∏è The Thousand-Yard Stare

Problem: Characters loaded with default forward gaze, not looking at each other

Looks like: Both staring into space, not interacting

Fix: ALWAYS adjust eye direction for each character

‚ö†Ô∏è The Cross-Eyed Look

Problem: Eyes rotated too far, looking past each other or crossed

Cause: Over-adjusting eye rotation values

Fix: Subtle adjustments (5-15¬∞ usually enough), check from camera view

‚ö†Ô∏è Mismatched Eyes

Problem: Left and right eyes looking in different directions (unless intentionally wall-eyed/lazy eye)

Cause: Adjusting only one eye

Fix: Both eyes should have similar rotation values

The Quick Eye Contact Test

After setting up eye directions:

  1. Switch to camera view (the view you'll render)
  2. Look at Character A's eyes
  3. Trace an imaginary line from their gaze direction
  4. Does it hit Character B (or intended target)?
  5. Repeat from Character B to A
  6. If lines connect properly = good eye contact!

‚úÖ Pro Eye Direction Workflow

  1. Position all characters in scene
  2. Set camera to final angle
  3. Starting with main character, adjust eyes toward conversation partner
  4. Switch to conversation partner, adjust eyes back
  5. Verify from camera view
  6. Make micro-adjustments
  7. Render test to verify (sometimes viewport lies!)
üëÅÔ∏è The Golden Rule: In a two-character scene, if they're interacting, their eyes MUST be properly directed. Nothing kills believability faster than characters staring past each other. Take the time to get this right‚Äîit's worth it!

Spatial Relationships and Personal Space

The distance and positioning between characters speaks volumes about their relationship. Cultural anthropologist Edward T. Hall identified distinct zones of personal space‚Äîlet's use them to enhance our scenes!

The Four Zones of Personal Space

Intimate Zone (0-18 inches / 0-45cm)

Who enters: Lovers, very close family, very close friends

When to use in 3D:

  • Romantic scenes (embrace, kiss, tender moment)
  • Comforting someone (hug, consolation)
  • Aggressive invasion (confrontation, threat)
  • Whispering secrets

Body language at this distance: Usually touching or nearly touching, very engaged

Personal Zone (1.5-4 feet / 45-120cm)

Who enters: Friends, friendly coworkers

When to use in 3D:

  • Friendly conversations
  • Casual interactions
  • Working together on a task
  • Most everyday social situations

Body language at this distance: Comfortable, open, engaged but not touching

Social Zone (4-12 feet / 1.2-3.6m)

Who enters: Acquaintances, professional interactions, casual strangers

When to use in 3D:

  • Professional meetings
  • Formal situations
  • New acquaintances
  • Polite interactions

Body language at this distance: More formal, reserved

Public Zone (12+ feet / 3.6m+)

Who enters: Public speaking, strangers, people being avoided

When to use in 3D:

  • Public speaking scenarios
  • Crowd scenes (people maintaining distance)
  • Showing avoidance or discomfort
  • Establishing shots (characters in environment)

Body language at this distance: Formal or disengaged

Height Relationships

Vertical positioning also communicates:

Same Height (Eye Level)

  • Meaning: Equals, peers, balanced relationship
  • When to use: Friends, partners, colleagues
  • How: Use figures of similar height, both standing or both sitting

One Higher Than Other

  • Meaning: Power dynamic, parent-child, teacher-student
  • When to use: Showing authority, dominance, or protection
  • How: One standing/one sitting, one on platform/stairs, adult-child

One Looking Down at Other

  • Meaning: Superiority, judgment, or care/concern
  • Context matters: Gentle look down = caring, harsh look down = judgment
  • How: One character standing over seated/kneeling other

Triangular Positioning (Three Characters)

For three-character scenes, positioning creates meaning:

  • Equilateral triangle: Three equals, balanced relationships
  • Isosceles with two close: Two allies, one outsider
  • Linear arrangement: One in middle = mediator or focus
  • Two facing one: Interrogation, confrontation, or presentation
üìè The Blocking Rule: In theater, "blocking" means where actors stand. In 3D, think like a theater director: every position has meaning. Characters don't just stand places randomly‚Äîtheir spatial relationships tell the story!

Composition with Multiple Figures

Multiple figures create compositional complexity. Let's master the principles that keep multi-character scenes visually compelling!

Focal Hierarchy

With multiple characters, you must decide: who does the viewer look at first?

üí° Creating Focal Priority

Primary focus (viewer looks here first):

  • Placed at rule of thirds intersection
  • Brightest lighting
  • Sharpest focus
  • Most detailed/expressive
  • Facing camera more directly

Secondary focus:

  • Slightly less prominent positioning
  • Reacting to primary character
  • Still well-lit and posed

Background characters: Present but not demanding attention

The Visual Triangle

Three characters naturally form a triangle‚Äîuse it!

  • Rule: Arrange three characters to form interesting triangle shapes
  • Why: Triangles are visually pleasing and stable
  • Variation: Mix heights and positions for dynamic triangles
  • Avoid: Perfect straight lines (boring, unnatural)

Overlapping and Layering

Don't line characters up side-by-side like a police lineup!

Good Overlapping:

  • Foreground character partially obscures midground character
  • Creates depth and visual interest
  • Guides eye through layers
  • More cinematic and professional

Bad: The Police Lineup

  • All characters side-by-side at same depth
  • Equal spacing between all
  • All facing forward
  • Result: Flat, amateur, boring

Negative Space Distribution

With multiple figures, managing empty space becomes crucial:

  • Don't spread evenly: Creates boring, symmetrical composition
  • Group and separate: Cluster some characters, leave space elsewhere
  • Create breathing room: Not every inch needs a character
  • Balance weight: Grouped characters on one side balanced by space or single character on other

Leading Lines and Gaze Direction

Use figures to guide viewer's eye:

  • Character A looks at Character B: Viewer follows gaze
  • Character points: Viewer looks where they point
  • Body orientation: Characters angled toward focal point
  • Arms and gestures: Create directional lines in composition

The Rule of Odds

Odd numbers of characters (3, 5, 7) often compose better than even numbers (2, 4, 6):

  • Why: Easier to create asymmetric, dynamic arrangements
  • Exception: Two characters work great for intimate scenes
  • Four or more: Group in odd-numbered clusters

‚úÖ Multi-Figure Composition Checklist

  • ‚ñ° Clear primary focal character
  • ‚ñ° Characters at varying depths (not lineup)
  • ‚ñ° Interesting shapes (triangles, diagonals)
  • ‚ñ° Balanced negative space
  • ‚ñ° Eye direction guides viewer through scene
  • ‚ñ° No perfect symmetry (unless intentional)
  • ‚ñ° Variety in character heights/positions
üì∏ Photographer's Tip: "Group photos look best when subjects are at different heights and depths. The same principle applies to 3D scenes. Variety creates visual interest‚Äîsameness creates boredom!"

Group Dynamics Principles

When you have three or more characters, group dynamics emerge. Understanding these helps you position characters meaningfully!

Common Group Configurations

The Circle (Discussion, Equality)

Setup: Characters arranged in circle or semi-circle

Meaning: Democratic discussion, equality, collaboration

Best for: Group meetings, friends gathered, councils

Poses: All facing inward, attention on each other or center object

The V-Formation (Leadership, Presentation)

Setup: One character at point, others behind/flanking

Meaning: Leader + followers, presenter + audience

Best for: Military, leadership, presentations

Poses: Leader facing forward/camera, others slightly behind

The Line (Journey, Unity)

Setup: Characters in line, facing same direction

Meaning: Shared journey, united purpose

Best for: Walking somewhere, viewing something together

Poses: All facing forward, varying slightly in position/posture

The Huddle (Conspiracy, Secrecy)

Setup: Characters very close, leaning in

Meaning: Sharing secrets, plotting, intimate group moment

Best for: Conspiracies, team planning, gossip

Poses: Heads close together, bodies angled inward

Two-Plus-One (Alliance + Outsider)

Setup: Two characters close, third separated

Meaning: Established pair + newcomer, or social exclusion

Best for: Love triangles, outsider storylines, choosing sides

Poses: Two engaged with each other, third apart or trying to engage

Power Dynamics in Groups

Power Structure Visual Indicators Positioning
Clear Leader One forward, others behind Leader at compositional focus, higher if possible
Equal Partnership Side by side, same height Balanced positioning, equal prominence
Hierarchy Staggered positions Most powerful forward/higher, descending order
Contested Leadership Two prominent, others watching Two opposed, others positioned between or watching

Crowd Scenes (5+ Characters)

Managing many characters requires strategy:

‚úÖ Crowd Scene Strategy

  1. Hero characters (1-3): Fully posed, detailed, best lighting
  2. Supporting characters (2-4): Well-posed, good placement, secondary lighting
  3. Background characters (remaining): Simple poses, less detail, fill space
  4. Vary heights and positions: Don't make everyone same height
  5. Create sub-groups: Clusters of 2-3 within larger crowd
  6. Some facing different directions: Not everyone watching same thing
  7. Depth variation: Foreground, midground, background layers

The Conversation Circle Principle

When multiple characters converse, they naturally arrange in ways that allow everyone to see everyone:

  • Three people: Triangle formation
  • Four people: Square or diamond
  • Five+ people: Circle or semi-circle
  • Key: No one has their back fully to another (unless intentionally excluded)
üé™ The Stage Director's View: "I always tell actors: the stage is not a democracy of space. Some positions are more powerful than others. Center stage is most powerful, downstage (close to audience) commands attention, upstage (far from audience) recedes. Use position to show relationships and power!"

Technical Management Tips

Let's cover practical technical aspects of working with multiple figures efficiently.

Workflow for Multi-Figure Scenes

Phase 1: Load and Position

  1. Load all figures needed
  2. Immediately move each away from origin
  3. Rename all descriptively
  4. Rough positioning in space (don't worry about exact poses yet)

Phase 2: Set Camera Angle

  1. Position camera for final composition
  2. This determines what you need to refine
  3. Characters out of frame need less work!

Phase 3: Pose Main Characters

  1. Fully pose and refine 1-3 primary characters
  2. These carry the story
  3. Include facial expressions, hand poses, everything

Phase 4: Pose Supporting Characters

  1. Good poses for secondary characters
  2. Less detail than heroes
  3. Focus on how they relate to main characters

Phase 5: Background Characters

  1. Simple, believable poses
  2. Enough to not look frozen
  3. May use preset poses with minor tweaks

Phase 6: Eye Directions and Fine-Tuning

  1. Set all eye directions
  2. Check for geometry intersections (clipping)
  3. Final compositional adjustments

Avoiding Geometry Intersections

Characters can "clip" through each other‚Äîhere's how to prevent it:

  • Check from multiple angles: Camera view might hide clipping visible from side
  • Pay attention to:
    • Hands going through bodies
    • Hair intersecting shoulders
    • Clothing poking through other characters
    • Feet sinking into ground or each other
  • Fix by: Slightly repositioning, adjusting pose, or using collision detection if available

Using Saved Poses for Efficiency

For background characters or common poses:

  • Use Poser's pose library presets
  • Save your own good poses for reuse
  • Modify presets slightly so not every background character is identical
  • Build a library of "conversation poses," "standing naturally poses," etc.

Performance Management

‚ö†Ô∏è Keeping Scenes Responsive

  • Lower preview quality: Document Display Style > lower quality
  • Hide figures not currently working on: Toggle visibility in hierarchy
  • Turn off shadows in preview: Speeds up viewport
  • Use simpler figures for distant background: Save detailed figures for heroes
  • Work in stages: Pose main characters, save, then add background

Saving Multi-Figure Scenes

Important practices:

  • Save incrementally: Scene_v1, Scene_v2, etc.
  • Save subsets: Save just the posed group as a preset for reuse
  • Document camera angles: If you have multiple good angles, save each as a camera
  • Test render early: Don't wait until everything is perfect to render a test
üíæ The Professional's Habit: Save versions frequently! Multi-figure scenes are complex. If something breaks or you want to try a different approach, you want to be able to go back to a working version!

Common Multi-Figure Scenarios

Let's walk through specific common scenarios you'll encounter!

Scenario 1: The Conversation (2 Characters)

Setup:

  • Personal distance (2-3 feet apart)
  • Facing each other at 30-45¬∞ angle
  • Both looking at each other
  • One gesturing (speaker), one listening pose
  • Open body language

Camera: 3/4 angle showing both faces

Story it tells: Friendly exchange, normal interaction

Scenario 2: The Confrontation (2 Characters)

Setup:

  • Close distance (intimate zone invasion)
  • Directly facing each other or very close to it
  • Intense direct eye contact
  • Tense poses (clenched fists, aggressive stances)
  • Possibly one pointing, other defensive

Camera: Side angle or over-shoulder to capture tension

Story it tells: Conflict, argument, challenge

Scenario 3: The Romance (2 Characters)

Setup:

  • Very close (intimate zone)
  • Bodies angled toward each other
  • Touching (hands held, embrace) or nearly touching
  • Soft mutual gaze
  • Relaxed, open poses

Camera: Close-up or medium shot capturing intimacy

Story it tells: Love, attraction, tenderness

Scenario 4: The Meeting (3 Characters)

Setup:

  • Triangle formation
  • Personal to social distance
  • All can see each other (no one's back fully to another)
  • Varied poses (not all identical)
  • Eye contact distributed (not all looking at same person unless they're presenting)

Camera: Angle capturing all three

Story it tells: Group discussion, collaboration

Scenario 5: The Leader and Followers (3+ Characters)

Setup:

  • Leader at front, higher if possible
  • Followers behind/flanking in V or line
  • Leader facing forward
  • Followers may look at leader or same direction as leader
  • Clear visual hierarchy

Camera: Slightly low angle emphasizing leader's prominence

Story it tells: Leadership, authority, following

Scenario 6: The Teaching Moment (2 Characters)

Setup:

  • Teacher gesturing/demonstrating
  • Student watching/learning pose
  • Both focused on object/task or teacher demonstrating
  • Personal distance
  • Student's attention on teacher or what teacher indicates

Camera: Side angle showing teacher's demonstration and student's attention

Story it tells: Teaching, learning, mentorship

Scenario 7: The Two-Plus-One Dynamic (3 Characters)

Setup:

  • Two characters close and engaged
  • Third character separated spatially
  • Close two looking at each other
  • Third looking at the pair or looking away
  • Clear visual separation

Camera: Composition emphasizing the separation

Story it tells: Outsider, love triangle, exclusion, or observer

Scenario 8: The Crowd (5+ Characters)

Setup:

  • 1-2 hero characters prominently featured
  • 2-3 supporting characters clearly visible
  • Remaining as background fill
  • Varied heights and positions
  • Sub-groups within crowd
  • Not everyone looking same direction

Camera: Wide enough to show crowd, composed to emphasize heroes

Story it tells: Public event, busy location, character in context of larger world

Project: Create a Three-Character Scene

Time to bring together everything you've learned!

üéØ Project Brief

Create a scene with three characters that tells a clear story about their relationships and what's happening.

Requirements:

  • Three distinct characters
  • Clear relationships evident through positioning and poses
  • Proper eye contact/gaze directions
  • Appropriate use of personal space zones
  • Good composition (triangle, depth, focal hierarchy)
  • All characters with appropriate expressions
  • Props supporting the scene (optional but encouraged)

Time allocation: 60-90 minutes

Choose Your Scenario

Pick one of these scenarios (or create your own):

Option A: The Job Interview

Characters: Interviewer, applicant, observer

Story: Tense interview moment

Key elements: Power dynamic (interviewer higher/forward), applicant nervous pose, observer watching

Option B: The Love Triangle

Characters: Two people interested in same person, that person

Story: Romantic tension

Key elements: Two looking at center character, center character must choose where to look

Option C: The Storyteller

Characters: Animated storyteller, two listeners

Story: Exciting story being told

Key elements: Storyteller gesturing dramatically, listeners engaged and reacting

Option D: The Disagreement

Characters: Two arguing, one trying to mediate

Story: Conflict with mediator

Key elements: Two facing each other intensely, third between them with calming gestures

Step-by-Step Guide

Phase 1: Planning (5 minutes)

  • Choose scenario
  • Decide: Who is each character? What are their roles?
  • Sketch or visualize spatial arrangement
  • Determine focal hierarchy (who's most important)

Phase 2: Load and Position (10 minutes)

  1. Load all three figures
  2. Immediately move apart (no overlapping)
  3. Rename descriptively
  4. Rough positioning in triangle or appropriate formation
  5. Set appropriate distances based on relationships

Phase 3: Camera Setup (5 minutes)

  1. Position camera for best view of scene
  2. Ensure all three visible (unless one intentionally hidden/background)
  3. Check composition‚Äîrule of thirds, triangle shapes
  4. This is your working view from now on

Phase 4: Pose Primary Character (15 minutes)

  1. Identify your main focal character
  2. Full pose‚Äîbody, hands, head tilt
  3. Expression matching scenario
  4. This character carries the scene

Phase 5: Pose Supporting Characters (25 minutes)

  1. Second character‚Äîpose in relation to first
    • Body oriented appropriately
    • Pose complements or contrasts main character
    • Expression appropriate
  2. Third character‚Äîcomplete the triangle
    • Position supports composition
    • Pose shows their role (mediator, observer, participant)
    • Expression appropriate

Phase 6: Eye Contact and Fine Details (15 minutes)

  1. Set all eye directions carefully
    • Who looks at whom?
    • Verify from camera view
    • Make sure eyes actually connect
  2. Check for geometry clipping
  3. Adjust any intersections
  4. Verify all expressions visible from camera

Phase 7: Polish (10 minutes)

  1. Add props if desired (chair, table, etc.)
  2. Check composition one more time
  3. Make any final micro-adjustments
  4. Save scene with descriptive name
  5. Optional: Quick test render
