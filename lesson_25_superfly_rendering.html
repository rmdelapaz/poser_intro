üöÄ What is SuperFly and Physically-Based Rendering?

SuperFly is Poser's implementation of Cycles, the same powerful render engine used in Blender. Unlike Firefly's artistic approach, SuperFly is a physically-based path tracer that simulates how light actually works in the real world. It's the difference between painting a picture of an apple and actually photographing one.

What is Physically-Based Rendering?

Physically-Based Rendering (PBR) means the render engine follows the laws of physics when calculating how light behaves. Every ray of light in your scene bounces around realistically, interacts with materials according to real-world properties, and produces results that match what a camera would capture.

The Path Tracing Process

Imagine you're trying to figure out what a photographer's camera sees. SuperFly shoots millions of "rays" backward from the camera into the scene. Each ray bounces off surfaces, absorbing some colors and reflecting others, until it either hits a light source or gets lost in the void. By averaging millions of these paths, SuperFly builds up an accurate image of what the camera sees‚Äîjust like real photography captures accumulated photons.

Why SuperFly Creates Realistic Images

Because SuperFly follows physics, several things happen automatically that you'd have to fake in Firefly:

üåà Automatic Color Bleeding

Place a red ball next to a white wall, and the wall naturally picks up a red tint. In the real world, red light bounces off the ball and illuminates nearby surfaces. SuperFly simulates this automatically‚Äîno special setup needed.

üíé Natural Caustics

Caustics are those beautiful light patterns you see through glass or water‚Äîlike the dancing light at the bottom of a swimming pool. SuperFly calculates these naturally because it traces how light refracts through transparent materials.

üîÜ Realistic Indirect Lighting

Light doesn't just come from light sources‚Äîit bounces off every surface, filling in shadows naturally. In a room lit by a window, SuperFly automatically calculates how light bounces off floors, walls, and furniture to illuminate the whole space. No manual fill lights needed!

‚ú® Accurate Material Interactions

When light hits a shiny metal, some reflects sharply while some absorbs into the material. When it hits frosted glass, it scatters. SuperFly handles all these interactions physically, so materials behave like they do in real life.

The Trade-Off: SuperFly's realism comes at a cost‚Äîtime. Because it's calculating millions of light paths, SuperFly renders take significantly longer than Firefly. A Firefly render that takes 3 minutes might take 20-30 minutes in SuperFly. But that extra time buys you physical accuracy and photorealism that's nearly impossible to achieve any other way.

SuperFly's Philosophy

The SuperFly Approach

SuperFly says: "Give me accurate material properties and realistic lighting, and I'll give you a physically accurate image." You're not manually painting with light and shadow‚Äîyou're setting up a virtual photography studio and letting physics do the work. This requires a different mindset than Firefly, but the results speak for themselves.

üí° Key Insight

SuperFly isn't "better" than Firefly‚Äîit's different. It's better for photorealism, architectural visualization, product renders, and situations where physical accuracy matters. But Firefly is better for character work, stylized art, animation (due to speed), and when you need total artistic control. Professional Poser artists use both engines strategically, choosing the right tool for each project.

Hybrid Workflow: Using Both

Many professional Poser artists don't choose one engine over the other‚Äîthey use both strategically:

The Smart Hybrid Approach

  1. Compose in Firefly: Test poses, camera angles, and basic lighting quickly
  2. Make decisions fast: Get client approval or make creative choices with fast Firefly renders
  3. Switch to SuperFly for finals: Once everything is locked in, convert materials to PBR and render in SuperFly for maximum quality
  4. Or split the scene: Render character in Firefly, environment/props in SuperFly, composite together

‚úÖ Pro Example Workflow

Project: Character wearing a diamond necklace in front of city skyline

  • Test render: Everything in Firefly (2 minutes) to verify pose and composition
  • Character: Render in Firefly (5 minutes)‚Äîfast, great skin, good hair
  • Diamond necklace: Render separately in SuperFly (20 minutes)‚Äîneeds caustics and realistic refraction
  • Background: Render in SuperFly with HDR (15 minutes)‚Äînatural lighting
  • Composite: Combine in Photoshop, adjust blending
  • Total time: ~45 minutes vs 60+ minutes if all SuperFly, with better results!

The Golden Rule: Use Firefly for everything until you have a specific reason to use SuperFly. SuperFly's power comes with complexity and time costs‚Äîonly pay those costs when you actually need what SuperFly offers. Don't use a sledgehammer when a regular hammer will do!

Understanding Samples

Samples are the heart of SuperFly rendering. Think of each sample as SuperFly shooting one ray from the camera through a pixel and tracing where it goes. The more rays you trace, the more accurate your average becomes, and the less noise you see.

How Samples Work

Imagine you're trying to figure out the average height of people in a crowd:

  • 100 samples: You measure 100 random people. Your average is rough‚Äîlots of variation possible.
  • 1000 samples: You measure 1000 people. Your average is much more accurate.
  • 5000 samples: You measure 5000 people. Your average is very accurate, but it took 50x longer than 100 samples.

Same principle in SuperFly. Each pixel needs enough samples to get an accurate color average. Complex lighting needs more samples than simple lighting.

Adaptive Sampling

Adaptive Sampling is a smart feature that stops calculating samples in areas that are already clean enough. Think of it like this: a bright blue sky converges quickly (becomes noise-free), but complex glass refractions take many more samples to clean up.

How Adaptive Sampling Helps

  • Simple areas: Stop at 500 samples when they're clean
  • Complex areas: Keep going up to max samples (2000) until clean
  • Result: You get clean renders faster because you're not wasting samples on already-converged areas
  • Adaptive Threshold: Lower values (0.01) are stricter, higher values (0.1) stop sooner

‚úÖ Recommended Adaptive Settings

  • Adaptive Threshold: 0.01-0.02 for final renders
  • Min Samples: 0 (let it adapt from the start)
  • Max Samples: 2000-3000 (the ceiling it can reach)

Bounce Settings Explained

Each type of light interaction gets its own bounce limit. This gives you fine control over render time versus quality for different effects.

Diffuse Bounces

Controls light bouncing off matte surfaces (walls, fabric, skin). Each bounce brightens the scene slightly as light fills in indirect areas. Recommendation: 4-6 bounces for interior scenes, 2-3 for simple outdoor scenes.

Glossy Bounces

Controls reflections bouncing between shiny surfaces. Important for scenes with mirrors or polished metal. Recommendation: 4-6 bounces for most scenes, 8+ if you have mirrors reflecting mirrors.

Transmission Bounces

Controls light passing through transparent materials like glass or water. Critical for realistic glass rendering. Recommendation: 8-12 bounces (glass needs multiple passes), reduce to 4-6 if you don't have transparent objects.

Max Bounces (Total)

The absolute maximum bounces for any light path regardless of type. Should be equal to or higher than your highest individual bounce setting. Recommendation: Set to 12 and let the individual bounce types control behavior.

Bounce strategy: Start with everything at 4 bounces. If glass looks wrong, increase transmission bounces. If indirect lighting seems weak, increase diffuse bounces. If reflections cut off in mirrors, increase glossy bounces. Target the problem rather than raising everything!

üé® Creating Physically-Based Surface Materials

SuperFly materials work fundamentally differently than Firefly materials. Instead of artistic parameters like "specular value," you use physical properties like "roughness" and "metalness" that describe how the material actually behaves in the real world. Let's learn how to create realistic PBR materials.

The PBR Material Model

Physically-Based Rendering uses a standard approach to materials that every surface in the real world can be described with just a few key properties:

Core PBR Properties

  • Base Color (Albedo): The true color of the material without lighting
  • Roughness: How smooth (0.0) or rough (1.0) the surface is
  • Metallic: Whether it's a metal (1.0) or non-metal (0.0)
  • Specular/Reflectivity: How reflective the surface is (for non-metals)
  • Normal Map: Surface detail and texture
  • Emission: If the material glows or emits light

Accessing the Material Room for SuperFly

‚úÖ Setting Up SuperFly Materials

  1. Go to Window > Material Room (or press Ctrl+M / Cmd+M)
  2. Select the object/material you want to edit
  3. In the Shader dropdown, look for Cycles shaders:

  4. For most work, use Principled BSDF‚Äîit's the Swiss Army knife of PBR materials

The Principled BSDF Shader

The Principled BSDF (Bidirectional Scattering Distribution Function) is your one-stop-shop for creating any material. It combines all the common material types‚Äîmetal, plastic, glass, skin, fabric‚Äîinto one flexible shader.

Creating Common Materials

Let's build several common material types from scratch using Principled BSDF:

ü™® Rough Plastic (Like LEGO)

  • Base Color: Your plastic color (e.g., red RGB: 0.8, 0.1, 0.1)
  • Roughness: 0.4-0.5
  • Metallic: 0.0
  • Specular: 0.5
  • IOR: 1.45

‚ú® Polished Metal (Chrome, Steel)

  • Base Color: White or slight tint (RGB: 0.95, 0.95, 0.95)
  • Roughness: 0.05-0.1 (very smooth)
  • Metallic: 1.0
  • Specular: Doesn't matter (metallic overrides)

  • Base Color: Gold color (RGB: 1.0, 0.78, 0.34)
  • Roughness: 0.1-0.2
  • Metallic: 1.0

ü™ü Clear Glass

  • Base Color: White (RGB: 1.0, 1.0, 1.0)
  • Roughness: 0.0
  • Metallic: 0.0
  • Transmission: 1.0
  • IOR: 1.5 (glass)

üß± Rough Wood

  • Base Color: Wood color or texture map
  • Roughness: 0.7-0.9
  • Metallic: 0.0
  • Normal Map: Wood grain texture
  • Specular: 0.3-0.4 (wood isn't very shiny)

üëï Fabric (Cotton T-Shirt)

  • Base Color: Fabric color or texture
  • Roughness: 0.9-1.0 (very matte)
  • Metallic: 0.0
  • Specular: 0.2-0.3 (minimal shine)
  • Normal Map: Fabric weave texture

üß¥ Skin (Human)

  • Base Color: Skin color or texture map
  • Roughness: 0.3-0.5

The PBR Golden Rule: Most non-metals should have Metallic = 0.0 and most metals should have Metallic = 1.0. There are very few materials that are "half metal." Roughness is where you control most of the look‚Äîsmooth for polished, rough for matte.

Understanding Roughness

Roughness is arguably the most important parameter in PBR. It controls how blurry reflections are:

Roughness Values and Their Effects

  • 0.0-0.1: Mirror-like, perfect reflections (chrome, polished metal, water)
  • 0.1-0.3: Glossy, sharp reflections with slight blur (polished wood, car paint)
  • 0.3-0.5: Semi-glossy, noticeable reflection blur (new plastic, satin fabric)
  • 0.5-0.7: Matte with hints of reflection (painted walls, some stones)
  • 0.7-0.9: Very matte, minimal reflection (concrete, old fabric, unfinished wood)
  • 0.9-1.0: Completely matte, no visible reflection (chalk, rough fabric, clay)

üí° Pro Tip: Roughness Maps

Real surfaces have varying roughness. A scratched phone screen isn't uniformly glossy‚Äîscratches are rough, the rest is smooth. Use a roughness map (grayscale texture) where black = smooth (0.0) and white = rough (1.0). This adds incredible realism by varying roughness across the surface.

The Metallic Parameter

Metallic fundamentally changes how materials reflect light. This is a binary choice in most cases:

Material Type Metallic Value Reflection Behavior
Metals (gold, silver, copper, iron) 1.0 Reflections are tinted by base color
Non-Metals (plastic, wood, skin, stone) 0.0 Reflections are neutral, base color only affects diffuse
Edge Cases (oxidized metal, metallic paint) 0.5-0.9 Partial metallic behavior (rare)

‚ö†Ô∏è Common Metal Mistakes

  • Dark metal base color: Metals should have bright base colors! Dark chrome looks dull because metal reflections carry the base color tint.
  • Too rough for polished metal: Polished chrome should be 0.05-0.1 roughness, not 0.5
  • Forgetting color tint: Gold is GOLD-COLORED in base color (RGB: 1.0, 0.78, 0.34), not white!

Texture Maps in PBR

PBR materials typically use multiple texture maps to create realistic surfaces:

Essential PBR Texture Maps

  • Base Color / Albedo Map: The diffuse color texture (connect to Base Color)
  • Roughness Map: Grayscale map controlling smoothness variation (connect to Roughness)
  • Normal Map: Surface detail and bumps (connect to Normal input)
  • Metallic Map: Which parts are metal (usually black/white, connect to Metallic)
  • Ambient Occlusion (AO): Baked shadows in crevices (multiply with Base Color)

‚úÖ Connecting Texture Maps

  1. In the Material Room, add an Image Map node
  2. Click the node, browse to your texture file
  3. Connect the Color output to the appropriate shader input:
    • Color texture ‚Üí Base Color
    • Roughness texture ‚Üí Roughness (use grayscale)
    • Normal map ‚Üí Normal (make sure it's marked as Normal Map)
  4. Adjust any multiplier values if the effect is too strong/weak

Quick Material Conversion: Firefly to SuperFly

If you have a Firefly material and want to approximate it in SuperFly:

  • Diffuse Color ‚Üí Base Color
  • Specular Value ‚Üí Use to estimate Roughness:
    • Low specular (0.1-0.3) = Rough (0.7-0.9)

üåÖ HDR and Environment-Based Lighting

One of SuperFly's greatest strengths is Image-Based Lighting (IBL) using High Dynamic Range (HDR) environment maps. Instead of manually placing multiple lights, you wrap your scene in a 360-degree photograph that provides realistic, natural lighting from all directions. It's like teleporting your 3D scene into a real-world location.

What is HDR Environment Lighting?

An HDR image is a special photograph that captures the full range of light in a scene‚Äîfrom deep shadows to bright sunlight‚Äîfar beyond what a regular image can store. When you use an HDR as an environment map, SuperFly uses the brightness information in that image to light your scene realistically.

How IBL Works

Imagine wrapping your 3D scene in a giant sphere that has a photograph printed on the inside. Every pixel in that photograph acts as a tiny light source. Bright areas (like the sun in the photo) emit strong light. Dark areas (like shadows under trees) emit weak light. The result? Your 3D object is lit exactly as if it were photographed in that real location.

Why HDR is Perfect for SuperFly

üåü Benefits of HDR Lighting

  • Instant Realism: Natural, believable lighting in seconds without manual light placement
  • Accurate Reflections: Shiny objects reflect the actual environment, not empty space
  • Color Accuracy: Lighting color matches real-world conditions (warm sunset, cool overcast, etc.)
  • Global Illumination: Light bounces naturally from all directions, filling in shadows realistically
  • Consistent Results: Same HDR = consistent lighting across multiple renders

The HDR Advantage: With Firefly, you might spend 30 minutes setting up a three-point light rig, tweaking intensities, and adjusting shadow softness. With SuperFly + HDR, you load one HDR file and get instant, photorealistic lighting. That's the power of environment-based lighting!

Setting Up HDR Lighting in Poser

‚úÖ Basic HDR Setup

  1. Make sure you're using SuperFly as your render engine
  2. Go to Render > Render Settings
  3. Look for the Environment or Background section
  4. Enable IBL (Image-Based Lighting)
  5. Click Browse and select your HDR file (.hdr or .exr format)
  6. Adjust IBL Intensity (start at 1.0, adjust to taste)
  7. Optional: Enable Background Visibility if you want the HDR visible as backdrop

‚úÖ Alternative Method: Environment Sphere

  1. Create an Environment Sphere prop (usually in Poser's props library)

üéØ Understanding Noise, Samples, and Denoising

One of the biggest challenges with SuperFly (and path tracing in general) is noise‚Äîthat grainy, speckled appearance in renders. Understanding why noise happens and how to control it is essential for getting clean, professional SuperFly renders without waiting days for a single image.

What is Render Noise?

Noise (also called grain or fireflies) is the random speckled pattern you see in undersampled renders. It happens because SuperFly is using random sampling to calculate lighting‚Äîlike polling random people to predict an election. Too few samples = inaccurate prediction = noise.

Why Path Tracing Creates Noise

Remember, SuperFly shoots random rays from the camera into the scene. Each ray bounces randomly until it hits a light or gets absorbed. With only a few rays per pixel, you get wildly different results for neighboring pixels‚Äîhence the speckled look. More rays = more consistent results = less noise.

The Sample Count Trade-off

Here's the fundamental challenge: more samples = cleaner image, but exponentially longer render times.

Sample Count vs Render Time

  • 100 samples: Very noisy, but renders in 5 minutes
  • 500 samples: Noticeable noise reduced, 15-20 minutes
  • 1000 samples: Good quality with some grain, 25-35 minutes
  • 2000 samples: Very clean, 45-70 minutes
  • 5000 samples: Almost perfect, 2-3 hours
  • 10000 samples: Diminishing returns, 4-6+ hours

The Law of Diminishing Returns: Going from 100 to 500 samples dramatically improves quality. Going from 2000 to 5000 samples gives minimal visible improvement but triples render time. There's a sweet spot around 1500-2500 samples where quality is excellent and time is reasonable‚Äîespecially with denoising.

Strategies for Reducing Noise

üìà Increase Samples (Brute Force)

Method: Raise max samples to 2000-5000

  • Pros: Guaranteed to work, mathematically perfect result
  • Cons: Very slow, time-consuming
  • When to use: Final portfolio pieces where time doesn't matter

üéõÔ∏è Adjust Light Paths

Method: Increase bounce counts for problem areas

  • Grainy shadows? Increase diffuse bounces to 6-8
  • Noisy reflections? Increase glossy bounces to 8
  • Grain on glass? Increase transmission bounces to 12
  • Pros: Targeted fix, doesn't waste samples everywhere
  • Cons: Still increases render time

üí° Simplify Lighting

Method: Make your lighting easier to sample

  • Use larger light sources (easier to hit with rays)
  • Avoid tiny, intense lights (create fireflies)
  • Use simpler HDRs without extreme bright spots
  • Pros: Can dramatically reduce noise at same sample count
  • Cons: May limit artistic choices

üé® Reduce Material Complexity

Method: Make materials easier to calculate

  • Reduce roughness variation (fewer blurry reflections)
  • Limit subsurface scattering (computationally expensive)
  • Simplify glass and refraction where not critical
  • Pros: Faster convergence, less noise
  • Cons: Reduces realism slightly

ü§ñ Use Denoising (The Smart Way)

Method: Let AI remove noise from lower-sample renders

  • Render at moderate samples (1000-2000)
  • Enable AI denoising in SuperFly settings
  • Get clean results in fraction of the time
  • Pros: Massive time savings, excellent results
  • Cons: Can soften fine details if overused
  • Best practice: This is the modern standard for SuperFly!

AI Denoising: The Game Changer

Denoising uses artificial intelligence to analyze your noisy render and intelligently remove grain while preserving detail. It's like magic‚Äîyou can get clean results at 1500 samples that would normally require 5000+ samples without denoising.

‚úÖ Enabling Denoising in SuperFly

  1. Go to Render Settings (SuperFly mode)
  2. Look for Denoising section (location varies by Poser version)
  3. Enable Denoising
  4. Choose denoiser type:
    • OpenImageDenoise (OIDN): Intel's AI denoiser, excellent quality
    • OptiX: NVIDIA's denoiser (requires NVIDIA GPU)
    • NLM (Non-Local Means): Older, less effective
  5. Recommended: OpenImageDenoise works great on all hardware
  6. Render normally‚Äîdenoising applies automatically

How Denoising Works

AI denoisers are trained on millions of images to recognize what's "real detail" versus "noise." They analyze patterns in your render:

What Denoising Preserves

  • Edges: Sharp boundaries between objects
  • Textures: Consistent patterns like fabric weave
  • Detail: Fine features with structure
  • Gradients: Smooth color transitions

What Denoising Removes

  • Random grain: Speckled noise with no pattern
  • Fireflies: Bright anomalous pixels
  • Color splotches: Random color variation
  • Shadow noise: Grain in dark areas

‚ö†Ô∏è Denoising Limitations

  • Can soften extreme detail: Very fine textures might blur slightly
  • Requires minimum samples: Below ~500 samples, denoising can create artifacts
  • Can't fix everything: Severe fireflies or structural problems need more samples
  • Not magic: Garbage in = garbage out. Start with reasonable sample count!

Optimal Workflow: Samples + Denoising

‚úÖ The Modern SuperFly Workflow

  1. Start with preview (200 samples, no denoising): Test composition and lighting fast
  2. Test render (500-1000 samples, denoising on): Check if materials and lighting work
  3. Final render (1500-2500 samples, denoising on): Professional quality in reasonable time
  4. Ultra quality (3000-5000 samples, denoising on or off): Only for portfolio showcase pieces

This workflow gives you 90% of the quality in 30% of the time compared to traditional "render at 10,000 samples" approach.

Dealing with Fireflies

Fireflies are those annoying bright white or colored pixels that show up randomly, especially around glass, caustics, or small bright lights. They're caused by rare, extremely bright light paths that got lucky.

Firefly Solutions

  • Clamp fireflies: In render settings, enable "Clamp Indirect" and set to 3-10 (limits brightness of indirect lighting)
  • Increase samples: More samples average out the extreme values
  • Enable denoising: Denoisers are good at removing fireflies
  • Simplify caustics: Reduce glass complexity or transmission bounces
  • Enlarge small lights: Tiny, intense lights cause more fireflies than larger, dimmer lights

‚ö° Advanced SuperFly Techniques

Once you've mastered the fundamentals, SuperFly offers several advanced features that can take your renders to the next level. These techniques leverage SuperFly's physically-based nature to create effects that are difficult or impossible in Firefly.

Subsurface Scattering (SSS)

Subsurface Scattering simulates light penetrating into a material, scattering inside it, and exiting at a different point. This is essential for realistic skin, wax, marble, jade, milk, and other translucent materials.

How SSS Works

Think of holding a flashlight against your hand in a dark room. You see a red glow around the light‚Äîthat's subsurface scattering. Light enters your skin, bounces around through blood and tissue, and exits nearby, creating that characteristic glow. SuperFly simulates this naturally.

‚úÖ Setting Up Subsurface Scattering

  1. Use Principled BSDF shader
  2. Set Subsurface value:
    • 0.0 = No SSS (opaque material)
    • 0.3-0.5 = Skin (subtle translucency)
    • 0.6-0.8 = Wax, jade (more translucent)
    • 0.9-1.0 = Very translucent (thin wax, backlit ears)
  3. Set Subsurface Color:
    • Skin: Reddish tint (blood color)
    • Wax: Yellowish/warm tint
    • Marble: White or material color
  4. Set Subsurface Radius (RGB):
    • Controls how far each color penetrates
    • Red penetrates deepest, blue least
    • Skin example: (1.0, 0.2, 0.1)

SSS Applications

  • Realistic Skin: Gives that living, luminous quality to characters
  • Ears: When backlit, ears should glow red‚ÄîSSS creates this automatically
  • Candles/Wax: Light glows through the material
  • Jade and Marble: Subtle internal depth and translucency
  • Food: Fruit, grapes, translucent vegetables

‚ö†Ô∏è SSS Performance Impact

Subsurface scattering is computationally expensive. It can significantly increase render times (20-50% longer). Use it only where it matters‚Äîvisible skin, translucent objects. Don't enable SSS on materials that won't show the effect (clothing, opaque objects).

Volumetric Effects (Fog, Smoke, Atmosphere)

SuperFly can render volumetric effects‚Äîparticipating media like fog, smoke, clouds, and atmospheric haze. These add depth and atmosphere to your scenes.

Types of Volumetric Effects

  • Homogeneous Volume: Uniform density (like fog in a room)
  • Heterogeneous Volume: Varying density (like clouds or smoke)
  • Emission Volume: Glowing volumes (like nebulae or magical effects)

‚úÖ Creating Simple Fog

  1. Create a large cube or sphere to contain the volume
  2. In Material Room, apply a Volume Shader
  3. Or use Principled Volume (easier option)
  4. Set Density:
    • 0.01-0.05 = Light fog/haze
    • 0.1-0.3 = Medium fog
    • 0.5-1.0 = Dense fog/clouds
  5. Set Color (white for fog, gray for smoke, colored for effects)
  6. Optional: Add texture for varying density (noise texture works great)

‚ö†Ô∏è Volumetric Rendering Warnings

  • Very slow: Volumetrics can double or triple render times
  • Noisy: Requires high sample counts (2000-5000) for clean results
  • Increase volume bounces: Set to 2-4 or higher for proper volume rendering
  • Use sparingly: Only when the effect is central to the image

Caustics: Light Through Glass and Water

Caustics are those beautiful patterns of focused light created when light passes through or reflects off curved surfaces‚Äîlike the dancing light patterns at the bottom of a swimming pool, or the bright spots under a glass of water.

Why SuperFly Does Caustics Naturally

Because SuperFly traces light paths physically, caustics happen automatically when you have refractive or reflective materials. No special setup needed‚Äîjust use proper glass materials (high transmission, correct IOR) and sufficient samples.

Optimizing for Caustics

  • Increase transmission bounces: 12-16 for complex glass
  • High sample count: Caustics are noisy, need 2500-5000 samples
  • Caustic photon mapping: Some Cycles versions support this for faster caustics
  • Bright lighting: Caustics need strong directional light to be visible

Caustics Example: Wine glass on a table with sunlight streaming through it. The focused light creates bright patterns on the table surface. In Firefly, this is nearly impossible. In SuperFly, it just works‚Äîbut requires patience for the samples to converge.

Displacement Mapping

Displacement actually moves the geometry of your mesh based on a texture map, creating real 3D detail. Unlike bump/normal maps which fake the appearance of depth, displacement creates genuine geometry.

‚úÖ Setting Up Displacement in SuperFly

  1. Your object needs high subdivision (low poly won't work)
  2. In Material Room, connect a displacement map to the Displacement input
  3. Use a Math node to control displacement strength:
    • Multiply displacement texture by 0.01-0.1 for subtle effects
    • Higher values = more extreme displacement
  4. Set displacement Method to "Displacement" (not "Bump")
  5. Adjust midpoint if needed (0.5 = neutral gray doesn't displace)

When to Use Displacement

  • Terrain: Mountains, landscapes, ground detail
  • Skin pores: Extreme close-ups where bump isn't enough
  • Fabric weave: Actual raised threads in cloth
  • Rough surfaces: Concrete, stucco, bark

‚ö†Ô∏è Displacement Cautions

  • Requires high geometry: Low-poly models will look faceted
  • Slow rendering: More geometry = longer render times
  • Memory intensive: Subdivided geometry uses lots of RAM
  • Can break silhouettes: Objects may extend beyond original boundaries

Shader Mixing: Combining Multiple Materials

SuperFly lets you mix shaders to create complex materials that combine properties. For example: metal with a layer of dust, car paint with metallic flakes, or wet surfaces with both matte and glossy properties.

Mix Shader Node

The Mix Shader node blends two shaders together based on a factor (0.0 = shader 1, 1.0 = shader 2, 0.5 = 50/50 mix).

‚úÖ Creating a Dusty Metal Surface

  1. Create two shaders:
    • Shader 1: Principled BSDF with Metallic = 1.0 (clean metal)
    • Shader 2: Principled BSDF with Roughness = 0.9 (dust/dirt)
  2. Add a Mix Shader node
  3. Connect both shaders to the Mix node inputs
  4. Use a texture map as the mix factor:
    • Black areas = clean metal shows through
    • White areas = dust covers surface
  5. Connect Mix Shader output to final Surface output

Shader Mixing Applications

  • Weathered materials: Mix clean and worn shaders with a dirt mask
  • Layered paint: Combine base paint with chipped/worn areas
  • Wet surfaces: Mix dry shader with glossy wet shader
  • Rust: Mix metal shader with rough rusty shader
  • Frosted glass: Mix clear glass with translucent rough glass

Camera Effects: Depth of Field

SuperFly supports true camera depth of field that simulates real lens behavior, creating beautiful bokeh (out-of-focus blur) effects.

‚úÖ Enabling Depth of Field

  1. Select your camera in the scene
  2. In camera properties, enable Depth of Field
  3. Set f-Stop:
    • f/1.4-f/2.8 = Very shallow DOF (portrait look)
    • f/4-f/8 = Moderate DOF (standard)
    • f/11-f/22 = Deep DOF (landscape)
  4. Set Focus Distance:
    • Use "Point At" to focus on subject
    • Or manually set distance in Poser units
  5. Render‚ÄîDOF is calculated automatically in SuperFly

üí° DOF Pro Tip

SuperFly's DOF creates realistic lens blur with proper bokeh shapes. For beauty shots of products or character portraits, use f/2.8-f/4 to create separation from the background while keeping the subject sharp. The out-of-focus areas will have that creamy, photographic quality.

Light Portals for Indoor Scenes

When rendering interior scenes with window lighting, light portals can dramatically reduce noise and speed up rendering. They tell SuperFly where light is entering the scene.

Setting Up Light Portals

  1. Create a plane object at each window
  2. In Material Room, set plane material to Portal shader (if available) or use a special setup
  3. Portal planes should face into the room
  4. Render‚ÄîSuperFly concentrates samples on portal areas, reducing noise

Note: Portal implementation varies by Poser version. Check your specific version's documentation.

Render Passes in SuperFly

Like Firefly, SuperFly supports render passes (also called AOVs - Arbitrary Output Variables) for advanced compositing:

Available SuperFly Passes

  • Diffuse: Diffuse lighting only
  • Glossy: Specular/reflections only

  • Starting with final render settings: Testing at 3000 samples wastes hours. Start low!
  • Changing multiple things at once: Can't tell what fixed or broke the render. Change one thing at a time.
  • Not saving render settings: Accidentally render at wrong settings and waste time
  • Ignoring denoising: Rendering at 5000 samples without denoising when 1500 + denoise would look the same
  • No render region for tests: Rendering entire image when only testing one material
  • Not watching first 100 samples: If it looks wrong early, it won't magically fix itself at 2000 samples‚Äîstop and fix it!

  • Lower samples for animation: 500-1000 samples with denoising is often acceptable for moving images
  • Lock all settings: Consistent render settings across all frames
  • Test one frame first: Make sure settings work before rendering 100 frames
  • Use network rendering: If available, distribute across multiple computers

  6. ‚úì Hide unnecessary objects: Objects not in frame should be hidden

üîß Troubleshooting SuperFly Renders

SuperFly's physically-based approach can sometimes produce unexpected results. Let's troubleshoot the most common issues and their solutions so you can diagnose and fix problems quickly.

Image Quality Problems

‚ùå Problem: Extremely noisy/grainy render even at high samples

Symptoms: Render still looks speckled after 2000+ samples

Common Causes & Solutions:

  • Denoising not enabled: Turn on AI denoising in render settings
  • Difficult lighting: Tiny bright lights or complex HDR with extreme contrasts. Use larger, softer lights or simpler HDRs
  • Complex materials: Too much roughness variation or glossy reflections. Simplify materials or increase glossy bounces
  • Indirect lighting issues: Increase diffuse bounces to 6-8
  • Volumetrics present: Fog/smoke needs extremely high samples (3000-5000+)

‚ùå Problem: Bright white/colored dots everywhere (fireflies)

Symptoms: Random bright pixels scattered across image

Solutions:

  • Enable firefly clamping: In render settings, set "Clamp Indirect" to 3-10
  • Increase samples: Fireflies average out with more samples
  • Enable denoising: AI denoisers remove most fireflies
  • Check for caustics: Glass with strong light sources creates fireflies. Increase transmission bounces or simplify glass
  • Reduce small bright lights: Replace tiny intense lights with larger softer ones

‚ùå Problem: Render is completely black

Symptoms: Nothing visible, just black image

Solutions:

  • No light sources: Add HDR environment or lights to the scene
  • HDR not loading: Check HDR path, make sure file exists
  • IBL intensity too low: Increase HDR/environment intensity to 1.0+
  • Camera inside geometry: Move camera outside objects
  • All materials black: Check base colors aren't set to pure black
  • Emission strength zero: If using emissive materials for lighting, increase emission value

‚ùå Problem: Render is extremely bright/washed out

Symptoms: Overexposed, blown-out highlights, no detail

Solutions:

  • HDR too intense: Lower environment/IBL intensity to 0.5-1.0
  • Too many lights: Reduce number or intensity of additional lights
  • Emission too high: If materials emit light, reduce emission values
  • Materials too bright: Check base colors‚Äîshould be 0.2-0.8 range, not pure white
  • No tone mapping: Enable tone mapping/exposure control if available

Material Problems

‚ùå Problem: Materials look flat/unrealistic

Symptoms: No depth, everything looks like plastic or painted surfaces

Solutions:

  • Missing roughness variation: Use roughness maps, not uniform values
  • No normal maps: Add normal/bump maps for surface detail
  • Wrong roughness values: Most real materials are 0.3-0.7, not 0.0 or 1.0 extremes
  • Missing AO: Ambient occlusion adds depth‚Äîuse it or bake it into textures
  • Poor lighting: Flat lighting creates flat-looking materials. Add directional light

‚ùå Problem: Glass appears opaque or black

Symptoms: Glass doesn't look transparent, appears as solid black or white

Solutions:

  • Transmission not set: Set Transmission to 1.0 in Principled BSDF
  • IOR incorrect: Set IOR to 1.5 (glass) or 1.33 (water)
  • Not enough transmission bounces: Increase to 8-12 in render settings
  • Roughness too high: Clear glass should have roughness near 0.0
  • Nothing to refract: Glass needs objects/environment behind it to show transparency
  • Base color too dark: Use white or very light color for glass base

‚ùå Problem: Metals look wrong/like plastic

Symptoms: Metal surfaces don't reflect properly, look fake

Solutions:

  • Metallic not set to 1.0: Metals need Metallic = 1.0
  • Base color too dark: Metals need bright base colors (0.8-1.0), not dark
  • Roughness too high: Polished metal should be 0.05-0.2, not 0.5+
  • No environment to reflect: Add HDR for realistic metal reflections
  • Wrong color for metal type: Gold = gold color, not white! Use proper metal colors

‚ùå Problem: Subsurface scattering looks wrong

Symptoms: Skin looks waxy, glowing, or completely opaque

Solutions:

  • SSS value too high: Keep subsurface at 0.3-0.5 for skin, not 1.0
  • Wrong subsurface color: Should be reddish for skin (blood tint)
  • Radius values incorrect: Red should penetrate deepest: try (1.0, 0.2, 0.1)
  • Not enough light: SSS needs strong backlight or sidelight to be visible
  • Too many samples needed: SSS requires 2000+ samples to look clean

Lighting Issues

‚ùå Problem: HDR not lighting the scene

Symptoms: Scene is dark even with HDR loaded

Solutions:

  • IBL not enabled: Enable Image-Based Lighting in render settings
  • HDR intensity too low: Increase to 1.0 or higher
  • Wrong file format: Make sure you're using .hdr or .exr, not regular .jpg
  • HDR not applied correctly: Check that HDR is connected to environment/background
  • Environment sphere inverted: If using manual sphere, make sure normals face inward

‚ùå Problem: Indirect lighting too weak/strong

Symptoms: Shadows too dark or scene unnaturally bright from bounced light

Solutions:

  • Too weak: Increase diffuse bounces to 6-8
  • Too strong: Reduce HDR intensity or decrease diffuse bounces
  • Color bleeding excessive: Reduce saturation of bright colored surfaces
  • Not enough samples: Indirect lighting needs adequate samples to converge

Performance Problems

‚ùå Problem: Render takes hours or never completes

Symptoms: Render progress is extremely slow or appears stuck

Solutions:

  • Sample count too high: Check if accidentally set to 50,000+. Use 1500-3000 for finals
  • Adaptive threshold too low: Set to 0.01-0.02, not 0.0001
  • All bounces maxed out: Don't need 16 bounces everywhere‚Äîuse 4-8
  • Heavy displacement: Displacement with high subdivision is very slow
  • Complex volumetrics: Reduce volume density or simplify volume shader
  • Too many polygons: Simplify geometry or use lower subdivision
  • Giant textures: 8K+ textures on everything wastes memory and time

‚ùå Problem: Out of memory errors/crashes

Symptoms: Poser crashes during render or shows memory errors

Solutions:

  • Reduce texture resolution: Use 2K or 4K max, not 8K everywhere
  • Lower displacement subdivision: Extremely high subdivision uses massive memory
  • Reduce scene complexity: Hide objects not in camera view
  • Close other programs: Free up system RAM
  • Switch to CPU if using GPU: GPU VRAM may be insufficient
  • Render at lower resolution: Reduce final output size
  • Render in tiles: Use render region to render parts separately, then combine

Unexpected Visual Artifacts

‚ùå Problem: Black spots or patches on surfaces

Symptoms: Random black areas that shouldn't be there

Solutions:

  • Incorrect normals: Normals facing wrong way. Recalculate or flip normals
  • Self-intersection: Overlapping geometry. Fix mesh or separate objects
  • Light leaks: In interior scenes, gaps in walls let light escape. Seal geometry
  • Not enough bounces: Light can't reach certain areas. Increase diffuse bounces
  • Ambient occlusion too strong: If using AO shader, reduce strength

‚ùå Problem: Objects casting no shadows

Symptoms: Objects appear to float, no shadow contact with ground

Solutions:

  • SuperFly calculates shadows automatically: Unlike Firefly, you don't enable/disable per light
  • Not enough light contrast: Increase HDR intensity or add directional light
  • Ground plane missing: Objects need surfaces to cast shadows on
  • Materials too bright: Overexposed scene washes out shadows
  • All lights too diffuse: Need some directional lighting for visible shadows

‚ùå Problem: Splotchy colors or incorrect color rendering

Symptoms: Colors look wrong, splotchy, or shift unexpectedly

Solutions:

  • Not enough samples: Color needs samples to converge. Increase to 1500+
  • Extreme color bleeding: Bright colored surfaces bounce color onto neighbors. Reduce saturation
  • Incorrect color space: Check if textures are in correct color space (sRGB vs Linear)
  • HDR color temperature wrong: Try different HDR or adjust white balance in post

Denoising Issues

‚ùå Problem: Denoising makes image blurry or removes detail

Symptoms: Textures look soft, fine detail is lost

Solutions:

  • Too few samples: Denoising works best at 1000+ samples. Below 500 creates artifacts
  • Denoiser too aggressive: Try different denoiser type (OIDN vs OptiX)
  • Render without denoising: Increase samples to 3000-5000 instead
  • Sharpen in post: Add slight sharpening in Photoshop after denoising

‚ùå Problem: Denoising creates weird artifacts or patterns

Symptoms: Strange blotches, smudges, or unnatural patterns after denoising

Solutions:

  • Sample count too low: Denoisers need minimum ~500 samples to work properly
  • Extreme noise: Scene is too difficult. Simplify lighting or increase samples first
  • Try different denoiser: Switch between OIDN, OptiX, or NLM options
  • Disable and re-render: Sometimes bugs occur‚Äîtry rendering again

Diagnostic Workflow

‚úÖ Systematic Troubleshooting Process

  1. Isolate the problem: Render a small region around the issue
  2. Check render settings:
    • Sample count reasonable? (1000-3000)
    • Bounces appropriate? (4-8 for most types)
    • Denoising enabled?
  3. Test materials:
    • Apply simple test material (gray, roughness 0.5)
    • If problem persists, it's not the material
    • If problem disappears, diagnose material settings
  4. Test lighting:
    • Replace complex lighting with simple HDR
    • Remove all additional lights temporarily
    • Gradually add complexity back to find culprit
  5. Simplify geometry:
    • Hide objects one by one to find problematic mesh
    • Check for overlapping geometry or inverted normals
  6. Check for user error:
    • Are you in SuperFly mode, not Firefly?
    • Is the correct camera selected?
    • Are objects on invisible layers?
  7. Render log: Check console/log for error messages or warnings

Troubleshooting Philosophy: SuperFly problems are almost always about one of three things: not enough samples (noise), incorrect material setup (physical properties wrong), or problematic lighting (too complex or intense). Fix these fundamentals before diving into obscure settings!
